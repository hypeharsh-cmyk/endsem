Chapter 5 Aggregate Supply and Demand

Book pp. 97–117 PDF pp. 115–135 Exam weight High Dornbusch · Fischer · Startz 11e

Chapter Highlights

  • Output and prices are determined jointly by aggregate supply and aggregate demand.
  • Short run: AS curve is flat (Keynesian) — demand determines output, not prices.
  • Long run: AS curve is vertical (Classical) — demand determines prices, not output.
  • The AS curve describes the economy's price adjustment mechanism over time.
  • Fiscal and monetary policy shift AD; only supply-side policies can permanently raise output.

1 · The Aggregate Supply Curve

Definition

Aggregate Supply (AS) curve — for each given price level, the quantity of output firms are willing to supply. It is upward-sloping because firms supply more at higher prices.

The AS curve describes how the price level adjusts over time in response to the gap between actual and potential output. It has two polar forms — classical and Keynesian — and a spectrum of intermediate slopes for medium-run horizons.

1.1 The Classical (Long-Run) AS Curve

Definition

Classical AS curve — vertical at $Y^*$ (potential/full-employment GDP). The same quantity is supplied regardless of the price level because the labour market always clears.

When demand is economy-wide, all factors are already employed. No additional labour or capital can be bid away from other sectors, so only the price level rises — not output. Potential GDP $Y^*$ grows over time as resources accumulate and technology improves, shifting the vertical line rightward, but this shift is exogenous to the price level.

Fig 5.1 — Classical & Keynesian AS Curves
Left: Keynesian (horizontal) — output determined by demand; price fixed.  |  Right: Classical (vertical) — output fixed at $Y^*$; price adjusts.

1.2 The Keynesian (Short-Run) AS Curve

Definition

Keynesian AS curve — horizontal at the current price level. Firms supply whatever quantity is demanded without changing prices, because there is unemployment and unit costs do not rise with output.

The intellectual genesis is the Great Depression: idle capital and labour could be put to work without raising costs. Modern framing: short-run price stickiness — firms are reluctant to reprice when demand shifts. Instead, they adjust quantities. Key implication:

Common Mistake
On a Keynesian AS curve, the price level does not depend on GDP. Ongoing inflation is explained by an upward shift of the AS curve — not by a movement along it.
Examiner Note
Questions often ask why AS is horizontal in the short run. The correct answer combines two ideas: (1) excess unemployment means firms can hire more labour at the current wage, keeping unit costs constant; and (2) short-run nominal price/wage rigidity (stickiness). A one-idea answer scores partial marks only.

1.3 Frictional Unemployment and the Natural Rate

Definition

Natural rate of unemployment — the rate of unemployment arising from normal labour market frictions (job search, reallocation, new entrants) when the labour market is in equilibrium. CBO estimate for the US: ~5.2%.

Even in the classical model — full employment of the willing-to-work labour force — there is always frictional unemployment. People take time to find the right job. So $Y^*$ corresponds not to zero unemployment, but to the natural rate.

2 · The Price Adjustment Mechanism

The AS curve is not just a static schedule — it describes how prices move dynamically toward equilibrium. The key equation:

$$P_{t+1} = P_t\bigl[1 + \lambda(Y - Y^*)\bigr]$$
Equation (1) — Aggregate Supply / Price Adjustment
Reading Equation (1)
1
If $Y > Y^*$ (boom): the term $\lambda(Y - Y^*) > 0$, so $P_{t+1} > P_t$ — prices rise next period.
Overemployment → firms bid up wages → cost-push → higher prices.
2
If $Y < Y^*$ (recession): $\lambda(Y - Y^*) < 0$, so $P_{t+1} < P_t$ — prices fall next period.
Slack → downward pressure on wages → prices fall (sticky in practice, slow).
3
Prices stop adjusting only when $Y = Y^*$ — the long-run equilibrium.
This is why the long-run AS is vertical at $Y^*$.
4
$\lambda$ controls the speed of price adjustment. Large $\lambda$ → fast return to $Y^*$ (classical emphasis). Small $\lambda$ → slow return (Keynesian emphasis, larger role for demand policy).
Definition

Output gap (GDP gap) $= Y - Y^*$. Positive gap (boom) → upward price pressure. Negative gap (recession) → downward pressure.

Fig 5.2 — AS-AD Equilibrium and Demand Shock
An increase in the money supply shifts AD rightward. In the short run output rises ($Y_0 \to Y'$) and prices rise slightly ($P_0 \to P'$). In the long run the AS curve rotates upward until $Y = Y^*$ and only the price level is higher.
Examiner Note
$\lambda$ (lambda) appears in many university exam questions. You must know that it parameterises adjustment speed, not the slope of a static curve. High $\lambda$ ≈ neoclassical world; low $\lambda$ ≈ Keynesian world. Policy relevance: if $\lambda$ is low, activist demand management is warranted.

3 · The Aggregate Demand Curve

Definition

Aggregate Demand (AD) curve — shows combinations of the price level and output at which both the goods market and the money market are simultaneously in equilibrium. Slopes downward.

The full derivation of the AD curve from the IS-LM framework comes in Chapter 10. Here we use the quantity theory as a short-cut.

3.1 Quantity Theory Derivation of AD

$$M \cdot V = P \cdot Y$$
Equation (2) — Quantity Theory of Money
From Quantity Theory to the AD Curve
1
$P \cdot Y$ = nominal GDP = total dollars spent. $M$ = money supply. $V$ = velocity (times a dollar turns over per year).
2
Assume $V$ is constant. Then $M \cdot \bar{V} = P \cdot Y$, giving: $Y = \dfrac{M\bar{V}}{P}$.
For fixed $M$, higher $P$ → lower $Y$: downward-sloping AD.
3
A 10% increase in $M$ shifts AD up by exactly 10%. At every output level $Y$, the price level consistent with equilibrium is 10% higher.
Because $P' = 1.1P_0$ restores $M'/P' = M_0/P_0$ — the same real money supply.

The deeper intuition: AD depends on the real money supply $\bar{M}/P$. High prices erode the real value of the money stock → lower investment → lower aggregate demand.

Micro vs. Macro Confusion — Common Trap
The AS-AD diagram looks identical to a microeconomic supply-demand graph, but the economics are completely different. In micro, the "price" is a relative price (exchange ratio). In macro, "price" is the nominal price level. And unlike micro supply curves, the macro AS curve is more elastic in the short run and less elastic (vertical) in the long run — the opposite of micro.

4 · Aggregate Demand Policy Under Alternative Supply Assumptions

Keynesian Case (Short Run)

AS is horizontal. Firms supply any amount at $P_0$.

AD shifts right → output rises from $Y_0$ to $Y'$. No effect on prices.

Government spending, tax cuts, or money supply expansion are effective at raising employment.

Classical Case (Long Run)

AS is vertical at $Y^*$. Firms always supply full-employment output.

AD shifts right → prices rise from $P_0$ to $P'$. No effect on output.

Demand expansion is entirely inflationary. Real variables unchanged.

4.2 Classical Case in Detail

Fig 5.4 — Classical Case: AD Expansion
Vertical AS at $Y^*$. AD shift drives prices from $P_0$ to $P'$; output stays at $Y^*$. Demand management is fully inflationary with no real effect.
Examiner Note
A common 10-mark question: "Using AS-AD analysis, evaluate the effectiveness of fiscal expansion under Keynesian and classical assumptions." Structure your answer as: (1) define both AS assumptions; (2) draw both diagrams; (3) trace the equilibrium shift in each; (4) interpret: output vs. price effects; (5) policy implication — when is demand management justified?

5 · Supply-Side Economics

All economists agree that policies genuinely shifting AS rightward — removing inefficient regulation, improving technology, legal infrastructure — are desirable. "Supply-side economics" in the political sense makes the additional claim that tax cuts are self-financing because the resulting growth raises the tax base enough to offset revenue loss. Dornbusch et al. label this "voodoo economics" (the phrase is George H.W. Bush's).

Fig 5.5 — Effects of Tax Rate Cuts on AS and AD
A tax cut causes a large rightward shift of AD (strong demand stimulus) and a small rightward shift of AS (weak incentive effect). Short-run result: $E \to E'$ — both output and prices rise. Long-run result: $E \to E''$ — a small permanent output gain at significantly higher prices. Tax revenues fall.
Examiner Trap — Supply-Side Claim
Never state that supply-side tax cuts raise tax revenues in a standard macro exam unless specifically asked to argue that position. The textbook evidence (US 1981–83) shows revenues fell. State: AD shift large, AS shift small → net effect is higher prices and modest output gain with a larger deficit.

6 · Long-Run Dynamics

Over decades, the long-run AS curve marches steadily rightward at the rate of potential GDP growth (roughly 2–4% per year). The position of the AD curve, driven mainly by money supply growth, determines the price level trajectory.

  • If AD grows faster than AS → persistent inflation (as in the US 1970s).
  • If AD growth matches AS growth → stable price level.
  • In the long run, output is supply-determined and prices are demand-determined relative to supply.
Examiner Note
The long-run insight — "prices are determined by the relative movement of AD vs. AS" — is the correct answer to questions like "What causes sustained inflation?" Demand growth exceeding potential output growth. This is the monetarist core: excess money growth → excess AD growth → inflation.

Formula & Concept Summary

Quick Reference

$P_{t+1} = P_t[1+\lambda(Y-Y^*)]$
Price adjustment (AS curve). $\lambda>0$ is adjustment speed.
$Y - Y^*$
Output gap / GDP gap. Positive = boom; negative = recession.
$M \cdot V = P \cdot Y$
Quantity theory of money. $V$ assumed constant → AD curve.
$\bar{M}/P$
Real money supply. Higher $P$ → lower real balances → lower AD.
Classical AS: $Y = Y^*$ for all $P$
Vertical. Full employment always. AD changes → only $P$ changes.
Keynesian AS: $P = P_0$ for all $Y$
Horizontal. Price fixed in SR. AD changes → only $Y$ changes.

Revision — Key Distinctions

  • Short run vs. long run: not two models — both are true at different horizons. Keynesian ≈ months; Classical ≈ decade+. The contested zone is several quarters to a few years.
  • AS shift vs. AS movement: Ongoing inflation = repeated upward shifts of horizontal SR-AS; not a slide along the curve.
  • $\lambda$ large: economy self-corrects quickly → less need for activist policy. $\lambda$ small: slow self-correction → Keynesian case for intervention.
  • Supply-side economics: small real AS effect, large AD effect, net inflationary with revenue loss. Do not confuse with genuine productivity-enhancing supply policies.
  • Potential GDP $Y^*$: grows exogenously over time (technology, capital, labour). Demand policy cannot permanently shift it.

Chapter 01
Aggregate Expenditure & Equilibrium Output
Reference: Dornbusch & Fischer — Chapters on Income-Expenditure Model & Multiplier
Expected Questions (10 Marks Each)
Question 1.1
What is the consumption function? Explain the determinants of consumption with the help of the Keynesian consumption function.
10 Marks
Definition
Consumption Function

The consumption function describes the relationship between household consumption (C) and disposable income (Yd). It shows how much of every additional rupee earned is spent on consumption.

Keynesian Consumption Function
C = a + b·Yd
a = autonomous consumption (spending even at zero income)  |  b = MPC (0 < b < 1)
Determinants of Consumption
  • Disposable Income (Yd): The primary determinant. As income rises, consumption rises — but by less than the rise in income (MPC < 1).
  • Autonomous Consumption (a): Consumption that happens regardless of income (e.g., using savings or borrowing). Determined by wealth, credit availability.
  • Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC): The fraction of each additional rupee of income that goes to consumption. A higher MPC means more consumption responsiveness.
  • Wealth: Higher wealth (financial assets, property) encourages greater consumption — the "wealth effect."
  • Consumer confidence & expectations: Optimistic expectations raise current consumption.
  • Interest rates: Higher interest rates discourage borrowing and encourage saving → lower consumption.
  • Credit availability: Easy credit allows households to borrow and consume beyond current income.
Graph — Consumption Function
Fig 1.1 — Keynesian Consumption Function: C = a + b·Y
C = a + bY (Consumption line)
45° reference line
Autonomous consumption (a)
Conclusion: The Keynesian consumption function shows that consumption has two parts — autonomous (independent of income) and induced (dependent on income via MPC). A higher MPC means consumption is more responsive to income changes, which also implies a larger multiplier effect in the economy.
Question 1.2
What is the output multiplier? Explain its working and how equilibrium is established in the goods market where real investment equals real savings.
10 Marks
The Output Multiplier
Output / Expenditure Multiplier

The output multiplier (k) measures how much equilibrium output (Y) changes in response to a unit change in autonomous spending. Because each round of spending creates income for others, the total effect on output is a multiple of the initial spending shock.

Multiplier Formula
k = 1 / (1 − MPC) = 1 / MPS
ΔY = k × ΔAutonomous Spending
MPS = Marginal Propensity to Save = 1 − MPC
How the Multiplier Works — Round-by-Round

Suppose autonomous investment rises by ₹100 crore and MPC = 0.8:

RoundNew Income GeneratedNew Consumption (MPC×Income)
Round 1₹100 cr₹80 cr
Round 2₹80 cr₹64 cr
Round 3₹64 cr₹51.2 cr
………
Total ΔY₹500 cr = ₹100 × 1/(1−0.8)
Goods Market Equilibrium: I = S

Equilibrium output is where planned aggregate expenditure = output. Equivalently, in a simple economy:

Equilibrium Condition
Y = C + I  →  Y − C = I  →  S = I
Planned investment = Planned savings ensures no unintended inventory changes

If planned I > S: output rises (positive multiplier effect). If planned I < S: output falls. Equilibrium is restored when S = I.

Graph — Keynesian Cross
Fig 1.2 — Keynesian Cross: Equilibrium where AE = Y
Conclusion: The multiplier amplifies any autonomous spending change into a larger change in output. Equilibrium is reached at Y* where planned AE = Y, equivalently S = I. A higher MPC → higher multiplier → larger output response to any spending shock.
Question 1.3
Explain the government spending multiplier. How does the inclusion of government change equilibrium output in the Keynesian model?
10 Marks
Government in the Keynesian Model

When government is included, aggregate expenditure becomes:

Aggregate Expenditure with Government
AE = C + I + G
C = a + b(Y − T)  where T = taxes
Equilibrium:  Y = [a + bT_0 + I + G] × 1/(1−b)
b = MPC  |  T = lump-sum taxes  |  G = government spending
Government Spending Multiplier vs Tax Multiplier
ConceptFormulaExample (MPC = 0.8)
Spending Multiplier1/(1−b)1/(1−0.8) = 5
Tax Multiplier−b/(1−b)−0.8/0.2 = −4
Balanced Budget Multiplier1ΔG = ΔT → ΔY = ΔG

The spending multiplier is larger (in absolute value) than the tax multiplier because a ₹1 tax cut raises income by ₹1 but only MPC×₹1 goes to consumption. A ₹1 of G directly adds ₹1 to demand.

Balanced Budget Multiplier — Key Result
Balanced Budget Multiplier = 1

If government increases spending (ΔG) and taxes (ΔT) by equal amounts, the net effect on output is ΔY = ΔG. Even a balanced budget expansion raises output — because the spending multiplier (5) exceeds the tax multiplier (4) in absolute terms.

Conclusion: Government injects spending into the economy, which is amplified by the spending multiplier. The government multiplier (1/(1−MPC)) is always greater than the tax multiplier (−MPC/(1−MPC)) in absolute value. This is why fiscal stimulus (via G) is more potent than equivalent tax cuts.

Money Demand & Supply

Expected Questions (10 Marks Each)
Question 2.1
What is money? Explain its functions, types, and why a barter system is inefficient compared to a monetary economy.
10 Marks
Barter System and Its Problems
Barter System

A barter system is a direct exchange of goods and services without a medium of exchange. It requires a "double coincidence of wants" — both parties must want exactly what the other offers.

Problems with barter: No common measure of value, indivisibility (you can't give half a cow), difficulty storing value, no deferred payment mechanism.

Functions of Money
  • Medium of Exchange: Eliminates double coincidence of wants — facilitates transactions.
  • Unit of Account: Common standard for measuring and comparing value of goods.
  • Store of Value: Wealth can be held in money form and used in the future.
  • Standard of Deferred Payment: Debts and contracts can be written in money terms.
Types of Money
TypeDescriptionExample
Commodity MoneyIntrinsic value = face valueGold, silver coins
Fiat MoneyGovt decree — no intrinsic valueCurrency notes, coins
Credit MoneyPromise to pay — bank liabilitiesCheques, bank deposits
Near MoneyHighly liquid non-cash assetsFDs, T-bills
Conclusion: Money solves all the inefficiencies of barter by providing a universally accepted medium of exchange, unit of account, and store of value. Its introduction dramatically reduces transaction costs and enables specialization and trade at scale.
Question 2.2
Explain the demand for money. Discuss the alternative views on money demand — transactions, precautionary, and speculative motives.
10 Marks
Three Motives for Holding Money (Keynes)
  • Transactions Motive: People hold money to finance day-to-day purchases. Increases with income (Y). This is the L1(Y) component of money demand.
  • Precautionary Motive: Held as a buffer against unexpected expenses (medical emergencies, car repairs). Also increases with income.
  • Speculative Motive: Held based on expectations about future interest rates. If interest rates are expected to rise (bond prices fall), people prefer to hold money rather than bonds. Inversely related to interest rate (i) — this is L2(i).
Money Demand Function
Liquidity Preference (Keynes)
L = L(Y, i) = kY − hi
k = income elasticity  |  h = interest elasticity  |  Both k,h > 0
Graph — Money Demand Curve
Fig 2.1 — Money Demand: downward sloping in i, shifts with Y
L(Y₁, i) — lower income
L(Y₂, i) — higher income shifts demand right
Conclusion: Money demand is negatively related to interest rate (speculative motive) and positively related to income (transactions + precautionary). The Keynesian liquidity preference function L = kY − hi captures this two-way relationship, forming the basis of the LM curve in IS-LM analysis.

Explain how money is created in the banking system. What is the money multiplier? Explain the monetary base, reserve ratio, and currency-deposit ratio.

Money Creation Process

Commercial banks create money through the credit multiplier process. When a bank receives a deposit, it keeps a fraction as reserve (reserve ratio = rr) and lends the rest. The borrower deposits this in another bank, which again lends (1−rr) of it, and so on.

Key Concepts
Money Multiplier and Monetary Base
Monetary Base (H) = Currency in circulation + Bank reserves
M = H × Money Multiplier
Money Multiplier = (cr + 1) / (cr + rr)
cr = currency-deposit ratio  |  rr = reserve-deposit ratio

Simple case (no cash leakage): Multiplier = 1/rr
e.g., rr = 10% → Multiplier = 10
Conclusion: Banks create money by lending out a fraction of deposits repeatedly. The money multiplier shows how a given monetary base supports a larger money supply. A lower reserve ratio or currency-deposit ratio → higher multiplier → more money created per rupee of base money.

10 Marks

Question 3.1
Explain the functions of a Central Bank (RBI). How does the central bank control money supply in the economy?
10 Marks
Functions of RBI (Central Bank)
  • Banker to the Government: Manages government accounts, borrowing, and debt.
  • Banker's Bank: Provides liquidity to commercial banks, acts as lender of last resort.
  • Currency Issuance: Sole authority to issue notes in India (except ₹1 coin — Govt of India).
  • Foreign Exchange Management: Manages India's forex reserves and exchange rate.
  • Monetary Policy Authority: Sets policy rates to control inflation and support growth.
  • Regulator of Financial System: Supervises commercial banks and NBFCs.
  • Developmental Role: Promotes financial inclusion and banking infrastructure.

Capital Adequacy & Leverage
Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)

Minimum capital a bank must hold as a % of risk-weighted assets. In India, Basel III norms require CAR ≥ 11.5%. Higher CAR → safer banks → limits excessive lending (leverage). Leverage = Total Assets / Equity Capital — high leverage amplifies both returns and risk.

Conclusion: Quantitative tools work through the price and quantity of money and credit overall, while qualitative tools direct credit to or away from specific sectors. Both together form a comprehensive monetary policy toolkit for the RBI to manage inflation, growth, and financial stability.

Chapter 04
Inflation — Measurement, Causes & Effects
Reference: Dornbusch & Fischer — Inflation Chapter
Expected Questions (10 Marks Each)
Question 4.1
What is inflation? Explain its types, causes, and measures to control it. Discuss the trade-offs involved.
10 Marks
Definition
Inflation

Inflation is a sustained and general rise in the price level of goods and services, measured by the rate of change of a price index (CPI or WPI). It erodes the purchasing power of money.

Types of Inflation
TypeDescription
Demand-Pull"Too much money chasing too few goods" — excess AD over AS
Cost-PushRising input costs (oil, wages) push up prices from supply side
Built-in (Wage-Price Spiral)Workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices → repeat
HyperinflationExtremely rapid inflation (e.g., Zimbabwe 2008)
StagflationHigh inflation + low growth + high unemployment together
Anticipated vs Unanticipated Inflation
Anticipated Inflation
  • Fully expected by agents
  • Costs: menu costs, shoe-leather costs
  • Can be indexed — less distortionary
Unanticipated Inflation
  • Not expected — catches people off guard
  • Redistributes wealth (borrowers gain, lenders lose)
  • Disrupts contracts, investment — more harmful
Measures to Control Inflation
  • Monetary Policy: RBI raises repo rate → credit contracts → demand falls → prices fall.
  • Fiscal Policy: Government cuts spending or raises taxes → reduces aggregate demand.
  • Supply-side measures: Boost production, reduce import duties on essential goods.
  • Price controls: Fixing maximum prices — effective short-term but creates shortages.
The Trade-off

Controlling inflation via tight monetary/fiscal policy slows growth and raises unemployment (Phillips Curve trade-off in the short run). Policymakers must balance price stability against output and employment goals.

Conclusion: Inflation arises from demand or supply shocks and has real costs — especially when unanticipated. Control requires coordinated monetary and fiscal policy, but tightening comes at the cost of lower growth. This trade-off is the central challenge of macroeconomic stabilization policy.

Exchange Rate

The exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. E.g., USD/INR = 83 means 1 USD = ₹83. It determines the relative price of traded goods between countries and affects exports, imports, and capital flows.

Question 5.3
Explain equilibrium output in an open economy. How does an open economy multiplier differ from a closed economy multiplier?
10 Marks
Open Economy Equilibrium

In an open economy, aggregate expenditure includes exports (X) and subtracts imports (M):

Open Economy AE
AE = C + I + G + (X − M)
Net Exports (NX) = X − M
Imports: M = M₀ + m·Y  (m = marginal propensity to import, 0 < m < 1)
Equilibrium: Y* = [Autonomous spending] × 1/(1 − b + m)
Open vs Closed Economy Multiplier
EconomyMultiplier FormulaExample (b=0.8, m=0.2)
Closed Economy1 / (1 − b)1 / (1 − 0.8) = 5
Open Economy1 / (1 − b + m)1 / (1 − 0.8 + 0.2) = 1/0.4 = 2.5
Why is the Open Economy Multiplier Smaller?

In an open economy, some of every additional rupee of income "leaks out" as imports — it generates income abroad rather than at home. This import leakage reduces the domestic multiplier effect. The more open the economy (higher m), the smaller the multiplier.

Conclusion: The open economy multiplier = 1/(1−b+m) is always smaller than the closed economy multiplier 1/(1−b) because imports represent a leakage of demand. Countries highly integrated into world trade have smaller fiscal multipliers — a key policy consideration for small open economies like India.
Derivative Questions
Derivative Q 5.D1
Given MPC = 0.75, MPM = 0.15. Calculate the open economy multiplier. If G rises by ₹200 crore, what is the change in equilibrium output? Compare with closed economy outcome.
10 Marks
Solution
Open multiplier = 1/(1 − 0.75 + 0.15) = 1/0.40 = 2.5
ΔY (open) = 2.5 × 200 = ₹500 crore

Closed multiplier = 1/(1 − 0.75) = 1/0.25 = 4

Types of Unemployment

FrictionalTime lag between jobs — search and matchingShort-termA software engineer between two jobs

Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU)
Natural Rate of Unemployment

The NRU = Frictional + Structural unemployment. It is the "full employment" unemployment rate — the level of unemployment consistent with stable inflation. Cyclical unemployment is zero at the NRU. Also called NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment).

Policy Implications
  • Frictional: Improve job information (employment exchanges, portals) to speed up matching.
  • Structural: Retraining programs, skill development, relocation subsidies.
  • Cyclical: Expansionary fiscal and monetary policy to boost aggregate demand.
Conclusion: Different types of unemployment require different policy responses. Monetary/fiscal stimulus only addresses cyclical unemployment — it cannot cure structural or frictional unemployment. Confusing the types leads to wrong policy prescriptions.
Question 6.2
Explain efficiency wage theory. How do minimum wages and labour unions affect unemployment? What role does imperfect information play in labour markets?
10 Marks
Efficiency Wage Theory
Efficiency Wages

Efficiency wage theory argues that firms deliberately pay wages above the market-clearing level because higher wages increase worker productivity — by reducing shirking, attracting better workers, lowering turnover, and improving morale. The result is that wages are sticky downward and unemployment persists even in equilibrium.

Reasons higher wages boost efficiency: reduce turnover costs, raise effort (workers fear job loss), attract higher-quality applicants, improve nutrition/health (in developing economies).

Minimum Wages & Labour Unions
Fig 6.1 — Minimum Wage above Equilibrium creates unemployment

When minimum wage (Wmin) is set above equilibrium (W*), labour supply exceeds labour demand — creating unemployment equal to (Ls − Ld). Similarly, labour unions bargain for wages above market clearing, benefiting union members but potentially causing unemployment for non-union workers.

Imperfect Information

Labour markets have asymmetric information — employers can't fully observe worker quality or effort before hiring. This leads to adverse selection (only low-quality workers accept offers at certain wages) and moral hazard (workers shirk once hired). Efficiency wages, signaling (education credentials), and probation periods are market responses to this information problem.

Conclusion: Labour markets don't always clear. Efficiency wages, minimum wage laws, union bargaining, and imperfect information all create wage rigidity — keeping wages above equilibrium and generating persistent unemployment. These are supply-side structural features that monetary/fiscal policy cannot easily fix.
Derivative Questions
Derivative Q 6.D1
Labour force = 500 million. Employed = 460 million. Of the unemployed, 15 million are frictionally unemployed, 10 million structurally, and the rest cyclically. What is the unemployment rate, NRU, and cyclical unemployment rate?
10 Marks
Solution
Total unemployed = 500 − 460 = 40 million
Unemployment rate = 40/500 = 8%

NRU = (Frictional + Structural) / Labour Force
NRU = (15 + 10) / 500 = 25/500 = 5%

Cyclical unemployment = 40 − 25 = 15 million
Cyclical unemployment rate = 15/500 = 3%

GDP Gap (Okun's Law approx): ≈ 2 × cyclical rate = ~6% below potential GDP
Answer: Unemployment rate = 8%, NRU = 5%, Cyclical = 3%. The economy is 3% below full employment, suggesting expansionary policy (fiscal/monetary) is warranted to close the output gap.

Why is SRAS upward sloping?
  • Sticky Wages: Nominal wages are fixed by contracts in the short run. When price level rises, real wages fall → firms hire more → output rises.
  • Sticky Prices: Firms don't immediately adjust all prices (menu costs, contracts). As demand rises, some firms produce more at existing prices.
  • Misperceptions: Producers confuse general price level rise with relative price rise and increase supply.

Question 7.2
What are the factors that shift the Aggregate Demand (AD) curve and the Aggregate Supply (AS) curve? Explain with graphs.
10 Marks
AD Curve — Why Downward Sloping?
  • Wealth Effect: Higher price level erodes real wealth → consumption falls.
  • Interest Rate Effect: Higher P → more money demanded → i rises → investment falls.
  • Exchange Rate Effect: Higher P → domestic goods costlier → exports fall, imports rise → NX falls.
Factors Shifting AD
AD Shifts Right (↑)
  • ↑ Government spending (G)
  • ↓ Taxes → ↑ Disposable income
  • ↑ Investment (optimism, ↓ interest rate)
  • ↑ Exports (foreign demand rises)
  • ↑ Money supply → lower interest rates
AD Shifts Left (↓)
  • ↓ Government spending (fiscal austerity)
  • ↑ Taxes → ↓ Disposable income
  • ↓ Consumer confidence
  • ↓ Exports (global slowdown)
  • ↑ Interest rates (tight monetary policy)
Factors Shifting SRAS
SRAS Shifts Right (↑ supply)
  • ↓ Input costs (oil price fall)
  • ↓ Wages (productivity rise)
  • Favourable supply shock (good harvest)
  • ↓ Business taxes / subsidies
SRAS Shifts Left (↓ supply)
  • ↑ Oil/input prices (supply shock)
  • ↑ Wages (wage-push inflation)
  • Natural disasters reducing capacity
  • ↑ Business regulations/costs
Graph — AD and SRAS Shifts
Fig 7.2 — Rightward shift of AD: P and Y both rise (SR); and leftward SRAS (supply shock)
Conclusion: AD shifts are driven by changes in C, I, G, NX, and money supply. SRAS shifts are driven by changes in input costs, wages, and productivity. A rightward AD shift raises both output and prices; a leftward SRAS shift creates stagflation (↑P, ↓Y) — the worst macroeconomic outcome.
Question 7.3
Explain the Phillips Curve. What is the trade-off between inflation and unemployment? How does the long-run Phillips Curve differ from the short-run?
10 Marks
The Phillips Curve
Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve (A.W. Phillips, 1958) describes an empirical inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation — when unemployment is low, wage demands push up prices (inflation rises); when unemployment is high, wage growth moderates (inflation falls).

Short-Run vs Long-Run Phillips Curve
FeatureShort-Run PCLong-Run PC
ShapeDownward sloping — trade-off existsVertical at NRU — no long-run trade-off
LogicExpectations not yet adjustedExpectations fully adjusted → real wages unchanged
Policy implicationLower unemployment via higher inflation — temporarilySustained stimulus only raises inflation, not employment
Friedman-Phelps Natural Rate Hypothesis

The long-run Phillips Curve is vertical at the Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU). Any attempt to permanently reduce unemployment below NRU via demand stimulus will only raise inflation — it will not reduce unemployment in the long run, as workers will eventually adjust wage expectations upward.

Graph — Phillips Curve (SR and LR)
Fig 7.3 — Short-run trade-off vs Long-run vertical Phillips Curve
Conclusion: The short-run Phillips Curve shows a real trade-off between inflation and unemployment. But the long-run PC is vertical — sustained demand stimulus can permanently raise inflation without reducing unemployment. Policymakers can exploit the SR trade-off but cannot permanently "buy" lower unemployment at the cost of higher inflation.
Derivative Questions
Derivative Q 7.D1
Using the AD-AS model, show the short-run and long-run effects of an expansionary monetary policy. What happens to P, Y, and interest rates?
10 Marks
Short Run vs Long Run Effects of Expansionary Monetary Policy

Initial state: Economy at E₁ with P₁, Y* (potential output).

Short Run: RBI increases money supply → lower interest rate → investment rises → AD shifts right → new equilibrium at E₂ with higher output (Y₂ > Y*) and higher prices (P₂ > P₁). Economy is above potential — inflationary gap.

Long Run: Workers demand higher wages to compensate for inflation → SRAS shifts left → SRAS intersects new AD at E₃, which is back at Y* but at an even higher price level P₃. Output returns to potential; only prices are permanently higher.

Summary of Effects
Short Run: ↑M → ↓i → ↑I → AD shifts right → Y↑, P↑
Long Run: ↑wages → SRAS shifts left → Y returns to Y*, P↑ further
Net LR result: Only inflation rises — output unchanged at Y*
Conclusion: Monetary expansion is effective in the short run (raises Y, lowers i) but is inflationary in the long run — output returns to potential while price level is permanently higher. This is the long-run neutrality of money.

Question 8.1
Explain the government budget. Distinguish between revenue deficit, fiscal deficit, and primary deficit. What role does fiscal policy play in stabilizing the economy?
10 Marks
Government Budget
Government Budget

The government budget is a statement of the government's expected revenues and expenditures over a financial year. Budget surplus = Revenue > Expenditure; Budget deficit = Expenditure > Revenue.

Types of Deficits
Deficit TypeFormulaWhat It Measures
Revenue DeficitRevenue Expenditure − Revenue ReceiptsGovt spending more on current expenses than it earns — dis-saving
Fiscal DeficitTotal Expenditure − Total Receipts (excl. borrowings)Total borrowing requirement of govt — most watched metric
Primary DeficitFiscal Deficit − Interest PaymentsCurrent fiscal position excluding past debt burden
Key Identity
Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments
If Primary Deficit = 0 → govt is not adding to debt in real terms (interest-adjusted)
India's fiscal deficit target (FRBM): 3% of GDP
Role of Fiscal Policy — Automatic Stabilizers
  • Recession: Tax revenues automatically fall (less income) → deficit rises → acts as stimulus. Unemployment benefits rise automatically — sustaining demand.
  • Boom: Tax revenues automatically rise → surplus → demand dampened. This is automatic stabilization — no legislative action needed.
Discretionary vs Automatic Fiscal Policy

Discretionary: Government actively changes G or T (budget decisions). Automatic: Progressive taxes and transfer payments that naturally expand/contract with the cycle — acting as built-in stabilizers without explicit policy action.

Conclusion: Fiscal policy is the government's primary lever for influencing aggregate demand. Deficits serve as useful counter-cyclical tools during recessions, but persistent deficits can crowd out private investment and raise debt burden. The primary deficit is the best indicator of the government's current fiscal health (net of inherited debt).
Question 8.2
What is crowding out? How does deficit financing by government affect private investment and interest rates? Explain using the IS-LM framework.

Crowding Out — Definition
Crowding Out

Crowding out occurs when government borrowing to finance deficit spending pushes up interest rates, which reduces private investment. The rise in G is partially (or fully) offset by a fall in private I — so the net effect on output is smaller than the simple multiplier suggests.

IS-LM Analysis of Crowding Out

Net effect: Output rises by less than the full multiplier. If LM is steeply sloped (inelastic money demand) → more crowding out. If LM is flat (elastic money demand / liquidity trap) → less crowding out.

Graph — Crowding Out via IS-LM
Fig 8.1 — Fiscal expansion: IS shifts right, but interest rate rises → partial crowding out
Conclusion: Crowding out reduces the effectiveness of fiscal policy. The IS shifts right (by the full multiplier), but the new equilibrium on the LM curve is at a higher i, which crowds out private investment. The actual increase in Y (Y₁→Y₂) is less than the simple Keynesian multiplier predicts. In a liquidity trap (flat LM), crowding out is zero and fiscal policy is fully effective.
Question 8.3
Distinguish between expansionary and contractionary fiscal policy. When should each be used? Explain with reference to the business cycle.

Types of Fiscal Policy
FeatureExpansionary Fiscal PolicyContractionary Fiscal Policy

ExampleIndia's COVID stimulus packages 2020-21Fiscal consolidation programs, FRBM targets
Fiscal Policy and the Business Cycle

Recession phase (Y < Y*): Use expansionary policy → ↑G or ↓T → shift AD right → close the output gap.

Boom phase (Y > Y*): Use contractionary policy → ↓G or ↑T → shift AD left → cool inflation.

Procyclical vs Counter-cyclical: Ideally fiscal policy should be counter-cyclical — stimulate in downturns, consolidate in booms. In practice, political pressures often make it procyclical (spend in booms, cut in recessions — worsening cycles).

Conclusion: Fiscal policy is a powerful stabilization tool that works by shifting aggregate demand. Expansionary policy closes deflationary gaps; contractionary policy closes inflationary gaps. The challenge is timing, size, and avoiding excessive debt accumulation — particularly important for emerging economies like India that need to balance growth with fiscal discipline.

Derivative Q 8.D1
Total government expenditure = ₹30 lakh crore. Total receipts (excl. borrowings) = ₹24 lakh crore. Interest payments = ₹8 lakh crore. Revenue expenditure = ₹22 lakh crore. Revenue receipts = ₹20 lakh crore. Calculate fiscal deficit, primary deficit, and revenue deficit.
10 Marks
Solution
Fiscal Deficit = Total Expenditure − Total Receipts (excl. borrowings)
= 30 − 24 = ₹6 lakh crore

Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit − Interest Payments
= 6 − 8 = −₹2 lakh crore (Primary Surplus!)

Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure − Revenue Receipts
= 22 − 20 = ₹2 lakh crore
Interpretation: Fiscal deficit = ₹6 lakh cr (borrowing requirement). Primary surplus = ₹2 lakh cr → government's current operations are actually generating surplus; entire deficit is due to past interest burden. Revenue deficit = ₹2 lakh cr → government dis-saving on current account.
Derivative Q 8.D2
MPC = 0.75. Government wants to reduce inflationary gap of ₹600 crore. How much should it cut spending? Alternatively, by how much should it raise taxes to achieve the same effect?
10 Marks
Solution
Spending multiplier = 1/(1−0.75) = 4
Tax multiplier = 0.75/0.25 = 3 (in absolute terms)

To close ₹600 crore gap via spending cut:
ΔG = −600/4 = −₹150 crore (spending cut needed)

To close ₹600 crore gap via tax hike:
ΔT = 600/3 = ₹200 crore (tax increase needed)

→ Spending cuts are more efficient: smaller ΔG achieves same output reduction as larger ΔT
Key Insight: To close the same inflationary gap, a spending cut of ₹150 cr works as well as a tax hike of ₹200 cr — because every rupee of G directly impacts demand, whereas a tax hike only partially dampens consumption (MPC fraction). Same asymmetry as the spending multiplier vs tax multiplier.

CH 01
/ Foundation · The five functions

Intro to Management.

Management is the process of getting things done through people, efficiently and effectively. That's the textbook line. The useful version: it's the art of turning resources into outcomes without burning the place down.

Henri Fayol broke this down into five functions that every manager performs, whether they're running McDonald's or a college fest. These five are the spine of your entire course — every caselet you'll see in the exam is testing one (or two) of them. Burn them in.

The five functions are Planning, Organising, Staffing, Leading (or Directing), and Controlling. Planning is deciding what to do. Organising is structuring who does what. Staffing is putting the right people in those slots. Leading is motivating and guiding them. Controlling is checking if the plan actually worked and fixing it when it doesn't.

"Please Only Sleep Late on Condoms."
Planning · Organising · Staffing · Leading · Controlling. Stupid, gross, unforgettable. If the caselet asks "which function of management is being performed," you scan this list in 5 seconds and pick.

Effectiveness vs Efficiency

This distinction shows up in every other caselet, so lock it in:

  • Effectiveness = doing the right thing. Hitting the goal. Reaching the destination.
  • Efficiency = doing it with minimum waste. Taking the shortest route.

A company can be effective but inefficient (hits the target but burns ₹10 crore doing it), or efficient but ineffective (uses minimal resources but for the wrong goal). Good management = both.

Levels of Management

The McDonald's example your prof used is gold. Two levels worth knowing for caselets:

  • Functional / Strategic level — the "brain." Decides standardised cooking processes, supplier contracts, reorder levels. Makes the blueprint.
  • Operational level — the "hands and legs." Frying fries at the right temp, cleaning tables, serving in 2 minutes, closing the till. Runs the blueprint.

Key caselet hook: functional decisions affect operations directly (raise orange prices → procurement, costs, pricing all shift). But operations can still fail even with a brilliant strategy if the mixer machine breaks down. Strategy is necessary, not sufficient.

If a caselet says "the manager decided strategy but execution failed on the floor" — it's testing the strategy/operations divide. Mention that operational staff cannot change strategy, but their failures can sink it.

CH 02
/ Structure · The skeleton

Organising.

Organising is what happens after you've planned. It's the process of figuring out who does what, who reports to whom, and how the work gets grouped. It's the function that builds the skeleton of the organisation.

The Organising Process

Whenever a caselet describes someone identifying activities, grouping them into departments, assigning duties, and setting up reporting lines — that's the organising function. The textbook steps are: identifying and dividing work, departmentalising (grouping similar activities), assigning duties to people, and establishing reporting relationships. You'll see this exact sequence in cases like Ishita organising the painting exhibition or Karan setting up the travel agency.

Functional vs Divisional Structure

Two big structures dominate the caselets. Knowing which one fits the case = easy marks.

Functional structure groups people by what they do — production, marketing, finance, HR. Best when the company makes one product or a small number of related products. Big advantage: deep specialisation, less duplication of effort, easier training. Big problem: people stop seeing the bigger picture, departments fight each other for resources.

Divisional structure groups people by product, region, or customer. Best when the company has diversified products (electronics + appliances + furniture, say). Each division is like a mini-company with its own production, marketing, finance. Big advantage: clear accountability, faster decisions, divisional heads develop general management skills. Big problem: duplication across divisions, and divisions fight each other for company resources.

Think of a college vs a multinational university.

A small college has one Math department, one English department, one Admin department. That's functional. Everyone studying English reports through the English HOD regardless of which course.

A multinational university like Manipal has whole separate campuses for Engineering, Medicine, Hotel Management — each with its OWN admin, faculty, finance team. That's divisional. The structure repeats per "product" (degree).

Formal vs Informal Organisation

Formal organisation is the official structure — the org chart, job descriptions, lines of authority. It's created deliberately by management. Communication flows through the scalar chain (top-down). It's rigid, predictable, and slow.

Informal organisation is what emerges naturally — the WhatsApp groups, the smoking-break friendships, the dramatic club at work. People interacting beyond their official roles. It's not on any chart. Communication flows in all directions (grapevine), it's fast, and it fulfils social needs employees can't get from formal structure.

Caselet hook: if the case talks about red tapism, procedural delays, low motivation, lack of social interaction — they want you to recommend introducing informal organisation alongside the formal one. If the case talks about chaos, rumours, lack of accountability — they want more formal structure.

Delegation, Centralisation, Decentralisation

This trio is the most heavily tested topic in the organising caselet set. Get it cold.

Delegation is one boss handing some authority to one subordinate. It has three elements that always go together: authority (the right to act), responsibility (the duty to perform), and accountability (the answerability for outcomes).

"Always Ride Anal" — every delegation needs all three.
Authority · Responsibility · Accountability. The trap caselets love: a manager gives a subordinate a job (responsibility) but no power to requisition tools (no authority). Subordinate fails. Question asks: can the manager blame him? Answer: NO, because responsibility without authority makes a subordinate ineffective. Manager remains accountable regardless (accountability cannot be delegated).

Centralisation means all decision-making sits at the top. The CEO/owner decides everything, even minor stuff. Good for small, stable companies. Bad when the company grows because decisions get bottlenecked at the top.

Decentralisation means decision-making authority is systematically dispersed throughout the organisation. Store managers can decide their own promotions, supervisors can approve refunds, regional heads can hire. It's not just one act of delegation — it's a policy.

Why decentralisation matters (the points caselets ask you to list):

  • Relief to top management — they focus on strategy, not minor decisions.
  • Develops initiative in subordinates — they learn to think and decide.
  • Develops managerial talent — the pipeline for future leaders.
  • Quick decision-making — local people decide locally, no waiting for HQ.
  • Better control — clear accountability at each level.
Tell-tale phrases for decentralisation in a caselet: "selectively disperse authority," "managers given autonomy," "decisions taken at lower levels," "store managers decide locally." See these → write decentralisation + 3-5 importance points.

McKinsey 7S Framework

This is the most "framework-y" thing in your syllabus. The 7S model says an organisation has seven interdependent elements that must align for it to perform. If any one S is weak, alignment breaks and the whole thing wobbles.

The seven elements split into Hard S's (tangible, easy to identify) and Soft S's (intangible, culture-driven):

  • Strategy — the plan to win in the market
  • Structure — how the org is arranged (hierarchy, reporting)
  • Systems — daily processes, procedures, IT
  • Shared Values — the culture, the "why we do what we do"
  • Style — leadership style of the top team
  • Staff — the people, demographics, skill profile
  • Skills — actual capabilities and competencies
"Sucking Six Sluts, Sometimes Standing, Sometimes Sitting."
Strategy · Structure · Systems · Shared Values (the middle one — the core) · Style · Staff · Skills. All seven start with S, all seven matter, Shared Values sits at the centre because culture binds the rest.

Think of the 7S as a band. Strategy is the setlist. Structure is who plays which instrument. Systems is the rehearsal schedule. Shared Values is the band's vibe — why they're in this together. Style is whether the lead singer is a tyrant or collaborative. Staff is who you've got — three guitarists and no drummer is a problem. Skills is whether they can actually play.

A great setlist (strategy) means nothing if the drummer quits (staff), or if everyone hates each other (shared values), or if the lead is a control freak (style). One weak S → chaos.

Three classic 7S-based questions to be ready for:

  1. Can a strong leader compensate for weak systems? Partially, short-term yes, but long-term no — leadership burns out without supporting systems.
  2. Can good strategy fail due to poor shared values? Yes — culture eats strategy for breakfast. Misaligned values cause execution to collapse.
  3. What happens if skills are high but structure is unclear? Talent gets wasted. People don't know who decides, who owns what, and brilliant individuals work at cross-purposes.
CH 03
/ Decisions · Rule or judgment

Programmed vs
Non-programmed.

Decision-making is the core of every management function. The distinction your prof drilled is: some decisions are routine and repeatable, others are one-off and complex. They need different techniques.

Programmed decisions are routine, repetitive, structured. The same situation comes up again and again, and there's a clear rule for handling it. Approving a customer refund within 30 days, reordering stock when it hits a threshold, calculating monthly office supplies budget — these don't need a meeting, they need a rule.

The techniques for programmed decisions are all about codifying the rule:

  • SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) — step-by-step instructions, like how a pharma plant sterilises machinery every morning.
  • Rule-based decisions — "if X, then Y." Retail return policies, for example.
  • Checklists — pilots use them pre-flight; nothing depends on memory.
  • Decision trees — branching logic. Bank loan: credit score >750 → approve; 650–750 → verify income; <650 → reject.
  • Routine budgeting — formula-based monthly allocations.
  • Automated systems — Amazon auto-reorders stock with no human in the loop.

Non-programmed decisions are novel, unstructured, complex. They've never come up exactly like this before. Should we launch 5G nationally? Should we pivot from B2C to B2B? Should we enter the Indian market? These need judgment, not rules.

Techniques for non-programmed decisions:

  • Delphi Method — experts submit opinions anonymously across multiple rounds until consensus emerges. Great for forecasting (5G adoption, future skill demand).
  • Nominal Group Technique — experts meet face-to-face, write ideas independently, share one by one, vote anonymously. Less time than Delphi, more structured than a normal meeting.
  • Experience — gut call from someone who's seen this movie before. A fintech founder pivoting based on years of banking experience.
  • Quantitative methods — Monte Carlo simulations, regression, scenario analysis. Used when you have data to model risk.
  • Quality Circles — small groups of workers identify and solve recurring problems. Common in manufacturing.
Caselet hook: if the case mentions a manager facing a completely new situation (entering a new country, launching a new tech), the answer is non-programmed + one of the techniques above. If the case is about a recurring situation (returns, reorders, payroll), it's programmed.
"Management is what happens between people at work every single day, not what's written in handbooks."
CH 04
/ People · Right people, right slots

Staffing.

Staffing is the function that makes sure the right number of right people are in the right place at the right time, doing the right things. It's HR, but with more managerial framing.

The Staffing Process

The full sequence, from forecasting to keeping people performing, runs in eight stages. You'll likely see the steps tested directly or asked about the order:

  1. Manpower / HR Planning — forecasting how many and what kind of people you'll need.
  2. Recruitment — generating a pool of candidates (campus, referrals, job portals, agencies).
  3. Selection — choosing from the pool (tests, interviews, assessments).
  4. Placement & Orientation — putting them in roles and onboarding them.
  5. Training & Development — building their skills (TCS's L&D centre is the textbook example).
  6. Remuneration — salary, bonuses, benefits.
  7. Performance Appraisal — evaluating how well they're doing.
  8. Promotion & Transfer — moving people up or sideways.
"Many Randy Sluts Pant Through Raw Performance, Panting."
Manpower planning · Recruitment · Selection · Placement · Training · Remuneration · Performance appraisal · Promotion. Eight steps, eight words. Disgusting and effective.

Manpower Planning Methods — The Formula Caselets

This is where staffing gets technical. Your prof gave you actual numerical caselets with these methods. Calculations are easy marks if you know the formula.

Bottom-Up Technique

You collect manpower requirements from each department at the lowest level and roll them up. Add them all, then apply organisation-wide adjustments (expansion %, automation reduction %).

Total = (Σ Departmental need) × (1 + net adjustment %)

Example: Departments need 80+50+20+30 = 180. Expansion +10%, automation −5%, net +5%. Final = 180 × 1.05 = 189.

Delphi Technique

Experts give independent estimates anonymously, you average them, run another round, re-average. The final number is the average of the last round.

Final estimate = average of last round of expert forecasts

Nominal Group Technique

Experts evaluate options face-to-face and assign scores. You compute a weighted average using the scores as weights.

Weighted requirement = Σ(Employees × Score) ÷ Σ(Scores)

Example: Plans A/B/C need 150/180/140 employees with scores 5/3/2. Weighted = (150×5 + 180×3 + 140×2) ÷ 10 = 1570 ÷ 10 = 157.

Ratio Analysis

You use a ratio of employees to workload as the base, then adjust for efficiency changes.

Employees needed = Expected output ÷ (Old productivity × (1 + efficiency gain))

Example: 1 employee handles 200 units, expected output 50,000 units, efficiency improves 10% → new productivity 220 units/employee → 50,000 ÷ 220 ≈ 228 employees.

Regression Analysis

You use a past statistical relationship between workforce and a driver variable (output, sales, doctors per population, whatever).

Y = a + bX (e.g., Y = 0.01 × Output)

Plug in the future X to get the future Y. If Y = 0.01X and X = 5,000 units, employees = 50.

Markov Analysis

This one looks scary but it's just probability matrices. You start with current workforce at each level (junior/senior/manager), and you have transition probabilities for what fraction stays, gets promoted, or exits. You multiply through to get next year's distribution.

Markov analysis is like predicting where students in a hostel will be next year.

If 70% of first-years stay first-years (re-take papers), 20% become second-years, and 10% leave… and you start with 100 first-years, then next year you'll have 70 first-years still in first year, 20 moving up to second year, and 10 gone. Do this for every level, add the promotions into the next level, and you've got next year's headcount distribution. It's just bookkeeping with probabilities.

Worked example from your slides:

  • 100 Juniors → 70 stay, 20 promoted to Senior, 10 exit.
  • 50 Seniors → 30 stay, 15 promoted to Manager, 5 exit.
  • 20 Managers → 16 stay, 4 exit.
  • Final: Juniors = 70, Seniors = 30 + 20 = 50, Managers = 16 + 15 = 31.

Internal vs External Supply

Once you know how many people you need, you decide where they come from:

  • Internal supply — transfers (sideways moves) and promotions (upward moves). Cheap, fast, motivating, but limited pool.
  • External supply — campus hiring, advertising, placement agencies, job portals, employee referrals, social media (LinkedIn). Wider pool, fresh blood, but expensive and slow.

The L&T Infotech case in your slides walks through this elaborately — campus = 25-50%, job portals = 70-90% for lateral hiring, consultancy 5-25%. If a caselet asks about recruitment channel choice, mention the trade-off between speed, cost, fit, and cultural alignment.

CH 05
/ The HR twist · Spence's insight

Signaling Theory.

Michael Spence's theory. The big idea: when employers can't directly observe a candidate's actual productivity, candidates use visible signals (degrees, certifications, prestigious institutions, work history) to communicate their underlying ability. The signal stands in for the unobservable.

This is a topic about information asymmetry. The employer knows what they want but can't see inside the candidate's head. The candidate knows their own capability but can't transmit it directly. So the candidate sends signals, and the employer reads them. A degree from IIM signals discipline, intelligence, and capability — even if the actual job doesn't require any IIM-specific content.

The catch your prof emphasised: signals don't necessarily increase actual productivity. They differentiate candidates based on perceived ability. This creates two real problems for HR:

  • Bias risk — over-relying on signals means favouring people from "top" institutions or "right" backgrounds regardless of job-fit. Talented candidates without those signals get filtered out.
  • Signal inflation — when everyone has a degree, the degree stops differentiating. So people pile on more credentials (MBA + certifications + courses) just to stand out, regardless of whether those credentials help them do the job.

Signaling is like Tinder bios. You can't actually verify if someone's a good partner from a profile, so they use signals — gym pics, job titles, "loves travel," dog photos. These signals don't guarantee they're a good partner, but they shift the perceived odds. Over time, everyone learns the signals and starts gaming them, so the signals stop being useful and you need new ones.

Hiring works the same way. Resumes are signal bundles. Some are real (skills, projects). Many are just signal theatre.

How modern HR fights signal-only hiring: by combining signals with actual evaluation — skill-based assessments, behavioural interviews, work simulations, case studies. Employer branding becomes a counter-signal too — companies signal their culture, growth, and values to attract real talent rather than just resume-builders.

If a caselet talks about hiring bias, over-reliance on degrees, or candidates being filtered by institution name — Signaling Theory is the lens. Mention information asymmetry, signals as imperfect proxies, and recommend combining signals with skill-based assessment.
CH 06
/ Influence · Get them to want it

Leadership.

Koontz and O'Donnell defined leadership as "the art of inducing subordinates to accomplish their assignments with zeal and confidence." Translation: it's the ability to get people to want to do things, not just be told to.

Your professor stressed an important contrast that almost always shows up in caselets: bosses vs leaders. Bosses use authority — they tell people what to do. Leaders use influence — they make people want to do it. Bosses manage tasks; leaders manage meaning. Ratan Tata responding to a street vendor during 26/11 was leadership, not management.

Characteristics of Leadership

  • Co-existence with followership — no leader exists without willing followers. Leadership is earned, not assigned.
  • Responsibility — the leader takes ownership of the group's success AND failure.
  • Understanding nature — sensing what the group feels and needs.
  • Precedence — the leader has to be competent and self-aware enough that others look up.
  • Situation — leadership patterns shift with context; what works in a crisis doesn't work in a calm.

Theories — Three Approaches

01 · Trait Approach

The oldest theory: leaders are born with certain traits. Keith Davis identified four:

  • Intelligence — leaders are generally smarter than the average follower (but not so much smarter that they can't relate).
  • Social maturity and breadth — emotionally stable, wide interests, secure self-concept.
  • Inner motivation and achievement drive — driven by intrinsic rewards more than extrinsic.
  • Human relations attitude — they respect followers, empathise, are employee-centred.

Critique: traits alone don't predict leadership. Lots of intelligent, mature people aren't leaders. Lots of leaders don't fit the trait list.

02 · Behavioural Approach

Forget traits — focus on what leaders do. Three behaviours:

  • Autocratic — commands, expects compliance, uses rewards/punishments. Quick, decisive, but kills initiative.
  • Participative / Supportive — assumes people want to contribute, allows initiative, gives support. Slower, but builds engagement.
  • Instrumental / Instrumental-Supportive — focuses on the rational manager role: planning, organising, controlling. Process-oriented.

03 · Situational / Contingency Approach

The most modern: the right leadership style depends on the situation. No one style works always.

Fiedler's Model says effectiveness depends on the fit between leadership style and three situational factors: leader-member relations (are you liked?), task structure (is the task clear?), and position power (do you have formal authority?). High control + favourable conditions favour task-oriented leaders; ambiguous middle situations favour relationship-oriented leaders.

Situational Leadership Theory (Hersey-Blanchard) focuses on follower readiness — the combination of ability and willingness. Four levels: unable + unwilling, unable + willing, able + unwilling, able + willing. As readiness grows, leadership shifts from directive to supportive to participative to delegating.

04 · Path-Goal Theory

The leader's job is to clear the path to the goal. Four behaviours:

  • Directive — tell them what to do (when tasks are ambiguous).
  • Supportive — show care (when tasks are stressful or repetitive).
  • Participative — consult them (when they have expertise).
  • Achievement-oriented — set challenging goals (when they have high need for achievement).

Path-Goal theory is like being a coach. If your player is new and lost, you give detailed instructions (directive). If they're stressed before a big game, you reassure them (supportive). If they're an experienced senior, you ask for their input (participative). If they're a star with high ambition, you push them harder (achievement-oriented). Same coach, different behaviour, based on what the player needs.

Leadership Styles (Kurt Lewin + Extensions)

This is the practical taxonomy that caselets test most. Six styles:

  • Autocratic — leader decides alone, no input. Fast decisions, but kills initiative, creates frustration.
  • Democratic / Participative — leader consults the group. Mutual acceptance of decisions, better attitudes. Slow, needs capable subordinates.
  • Laissez-Faire / Free Rein — leader steps back, group decides. Great when subordinates are highly skilled and self-motivated. Risky — control collapses if they're not.
  • Functional — leader is a domain expert. Subordinates learn skills, but the expert may not connect with the average worker.
  • Institutional — leader is in charge because of position/title, not expertise. Has official authority but may lack moral authority or domain knowledge.
  • Paternalistic — leader acts like a parent. Takes care of subordinates' welfare. Good for loyalty, but limits subordinates' intellectual autonomy.
"All Dirty Lads Fuck In Parties."
Autocratic · Democratic · Laissez-Faire · Functional · Institutional · Paternalistic. If a caselet describes leader behaviour, run through this list, pick the closest match, justify it.

Contemporary Theories

Transactional leaders are exchange-based — clarify roles, set rewards for performance, fix problems. Stable, predictable, good for steady-state operations. They keep the machine running.

Charismatic leaders use personal magnetism and vision to inspire people to do more than they normally would. Unpredictable, atmosphere of change, sometimes obsessed by their own vision. Think Dhirubhai Ambani.

Transformational leaders are charismatic with substance — they recognise followers' deeper needs, help them see old problems in new ways, encourage them to question the status quo. They don't just inspire, they transform followers into people who can lead themselves. Think Howard Schultz at Starbucks, or Narayana Murthy.

If a caselet shows a leader who only manages tasks and rewards = transactional. If a leader inspires through vision and personal charm = charismatic. If a leader inspires AND grows their people into better versions of themselves = transformational.

CH 07
/ Check & Correct · Close the loop

Controlling.

Controlling is the management function that checks whether actual performance matches planned performance — and corrects deviations. It's the closing of the loop. Planning sets the goals; controlling makes sure you hit them.

The link between planning and controlling is intimate. Planning without controlling is wishful thinking. Controlling without planning is meaningless because you'd have no standard to compare against. They're the bookends of execution.

The Control Process — Five Steps

  1. Setting objectives — what are we trying to achieve?
  2. Establishing standards — measurable targets (quantitative like units/sales, or qualitative like satisfaction/morale).
  3. Measuring actual performance — through observation, statistical reports, oral or written reports, sample checking.
  4. Comparing actual vs standard — calculating the deviation.
  5. Taking corrective action — three choices: do nothing (if deviation is acceptable), correct performance (immediate or basic), or revise the standard (if it was unrealistic).
"Sexy Sluts Measure Cocks Carefully."
Set objectives · Standards · Measure · Compare · Correct. Five steps, in order. If the caselet asks "which step of controlling is being shown," you scan this and identify.

Two Principles of Managerial Control

These two get tested all the time — they're how managers handle the avalanche of data without going mad.

Critical Point Control says don't waste time monitoring everything. Focus on Key Result Areas (KRAs) — the few critical points where failure would seriously hurt the business. A production manager should focus on machine performance and quality, not on minor stationery expenses.

Management by Exception (MBE) says only significant deviations beyond a permissible limit get escalated to management. If raw material costs rise 2% and that's within acceptable range, ignore it. If they rise 5%, escalate. Don't drown leaders in data; surface only what needs decisions.

MBE is like notifications on your phone. If every app notified you about everything, you'd ignore your phone entirely. So you set rules — only important emails ping, only certain messages buzz, only specific people break through Do Not Disturb. The threshold is the "acceptable range." Anything that crosses the threshold = exception = your attention.

Types of Control — By Timing

Three types based on when the control happens relative to the activity:

  • Feedforward control — before the activity. Prevents problems by setting up the right conditions. Quality checks on incoming raw materials, ISO supplier requirements, design reviews. The cheapest form of control.
  • Concurrent control — during the activity. Real-time monitoring, direct supervision, "management by walking around." Catches problems as they happen.
  • Feedback control — after the activity. Examines results, takes corrective action for next time. Annual appraisals, post-mortem reviews. Useful for learning, but the damage is already done.
"Before, During, After — Foreplay, Fucking, Feedback."
Feedforward (before) · Concurrent (during) · Feedback (after). Three Fs, three phases. Caselet with "supplier quality checks weak" → feedforward failure. "No real-time monitoring during production" → concurrent failure. "Defects found only after shipment" → feedback-only system, weak prevention.

Tools for Controlling Performance

Financial Controls

Traditional tools: Ratio analysis (liquidity, leverage, activity, profitability), budget analysis (quantitative standards and deviations).

Modern tools:

  • Economic Value Added (EVA) — value created by the company's assets beyond the cost of capital. The "are we actually earning more than what we'd earn if we just invested the money?" measure.
  • Market Value Added (MVA) — the value the stock market places on the company's investments. If market value > invested capital → managers have created wealth. If <, they've destroyed it.

EVA is like comparing your business return to your alternative. If you put ₹100 into your shop and earned ₹8, but you could've parked it in an FD at 7%, your EVA is only ₹1. You created ₹1 of real value beyond the alternative. If the FD was 9%, your EVA would be NEGATIVE — you destroyed value by running the shop.

MVA is like asking: "If I tried to sell the whole shop today, would I get more than I put in?" If yes, wealth was created. If no, even if I'm profitable on paper, the market doesn't believe in me.

Balanced Scorecard

Argues that finance alone is too narrow. Track four dimensions equally:

  • Financial — revenue, profit, costs.
  • Customer — satisfaction, retention, share.
  • Internal processes — efficiency, quality, cycle time.
  • Learning & growth — people, innovation, skills.

Balance matters — overweight finance and you cut training, damage culture, lose long-term capacity.

Information Controls & MIS

Managers need the right info at the right time in the right amount. MIS (Management Information System) is the system that turns raw data into meaningful information. Data is an unsorted customer list; information is "your top 10% customers by lifetime value." Information is data with meaning.

Benchmarking

Comparing your performance against the best practices of competitors or non-competitors to identify gaps and improve. Three types:

  • Internal benchmarking — comparing across your own plants/divisions.
  • Competitive benchmarking — studying direct rivals.
  • Functional benchmarking — borrowing best practices from totally different industries (e.g., a hospital learning from an airline how to manage scheduling).

Performance & Effectiveness Models

When evaluating organisational effectiveness, three lenses:

  • Systems resource model — how well does the firm acquire scarce resources from its environment? Use this for cases about talent wars, raw material shortages, capital access.
  • Process model — how efficiently does the firm convert inputs into outputs? Use this for production delays, quality issues, throughput problems.
  • Multiple constituencies model — how well does the firm meet ALL stakeholder needs? Use this when caselets mention conflicting demands from customers, employees, investors, government.

Limitations of Controlling

Important for any "why is control failing" caselet:

  • Difficult to set quantitative standards for qualitative things (motivation, satisfaction).
  • Controlling is expensive — surveillance, audits, reporting all cost time and money.
  • Resistance from employees — people don't like being monitored. CCTV and tracking can feel invasive.
  • Little control over external factors — competitors, regulations, market shifts.
CH 08
/ The integrator · Lived, not designed

Management in Action.

This is the "so what?" chapter — the integrator. The big idea: in real organisations, there's always a gap between what's written (formal policies, org charts, manuals) and what's actually lived (the daily reality of how work gets done). Management success depends on the lived reality, not the documents.

Your professor's point is that a company can have perfect policies, structured hierarchy, defined processes — and still fail. Why? Because the five functions get executed in tiny everyday interactions, not in corporate documents. Management is lived, not designed.

How Each Function Plays Out in Real Life

Planning in daily reality isn't long-term strategy documents — it's whether your manager clearly tells you what's expected this week, whether deadlines are realistic, whether expectations are actually communicated. Unclear planning at the employee level creates confusion and rework even if the corporate plan looks beautiful.

Organising in practice isn't the org chart — it's whether work is distributed well, communication flows between levels, teams collaborate informally when needed. A well-charted organisation can still fail if coordination is weak or if informal networks dominate without alignment.

Staffing in real workplaces isn't just hiring — it's onboarding quality, training depth, role clarity, mentoring. People struggle not because they're unskilled but because they're poorly integrated into the system.

Leadership in action is daily manager behaviour — how they communicate, support during pressure, motivate. A strong leader can compensate for weak systems. A weak leader makes even a great system pointless.

Control in real time isn't annual appraisal — it's continuous feedback, early correction, fairness in evaluation. Delayed or biased control kills motivation faster than no control.

If a caselet describes a "perfect on paper, broken in practice" company → this is the lens. Mention the gap between formal system and lived reality. Recommend strengthening daily execution of the five functions.

They fail because expectations are unclear."

CASELET 01 · MOTIVATION

The Overloaded Star

"Ravi is the team's top performer. Lately he's been missing deadlines and seems withdrawn. Neha shows up but contributes the bare minimum. Arjun is a high-potential employee threatening to leave. The manager wonders why one motivation policy isn't working for all three."
DiagnosisThree different motivation problems — burnout (Ravi), disengagement (Neha), growth frustration (Arjun). Tests whether you can distinguish root causes. Concepts to useIntrinsic vs extrinsic motivation; adaptive management; situational leadership; recognising that uniform interventions worsen at least one case. Structure(1) Identify root cause for each person. (2) Recommend tailored intervention — workload reduction for Ravi, ownership task for Neha, stretch assignment for Arjun. (3) Justify why one-size-fits-all fails. (4) Rank by urgency: Ravi first (burnout risk), Arjun next (attrition), Neha last (stable but stagnant).
CASELET 02 · CONTROLLING

Targets Missed at TechCorp

"A company set quarterly targets of 50 projects with 95% on-time completion and 4/5 satisfaction. Actual: 38 projects, 5.5-week average delivery, 3.6/5 satisfaction. Team leaders blame unrealistic timelines, coordination issues, and market complexity."
DiagnosisClassic 5-step control process caselet with a variance. Concepts to useThe five steps of control; deviation analysis; Critical Point Control; Management by Exception; corrective action vs revising standards. Structure(1) State the objective, standard, actual performance, and variance clearly. (2) Conclude standard NOT attained, variance significant. (3) Identify internal causes (skills, coordination) and external causes (complexity). (4) Recommend corrective actions first; only revise standards if genuinely unrealistic.
CASELET 03 · LEADERSHIP

The Silent Supervisor

"Ramesh is technically strong but never communicates clearly. Workers complain instructions like 'finish quickly' are vague. Output is up, but rejection rate is also up. Ramesh insists workers should figure it out themselves."
DiagnosisLeadership failure in communication + control gap (no clear standards). Concepts to usePath-Goal theory (directive behaviour needed when tasks are ambiguous); concurrent vs feedforward control; setting clear standards before measuring performance. Structure(1) Identify the dual failure — leadership communication + missing performance standards. (2) Recommend: define quality parameters, daily briefings, demonstration + feedback. (3) Close with the line — "people don't fail because they're incapable; they fail because expectations are unclear."
CASELET 04 · CONTROL

The Over-Controlling Manager

"A manager introduced daily reporting, frequent inspections, and penalties. Errors dropped initially. After 3 months: morale dropped, productivity declined, employees stopped showing initiative. Manager argues 'control is necessary.'"
DiagnosisOver-control / micromanagement / punitive control system. Concepts to useLimitations of controlling (employee resistance); balance between autonomy and accountability; concurrent vs feedforward; Critical Point Control (focus on what matters, not everything). Structure(1) Differentiate control from over-control. (2) Explain how excess punitive control kills initiative and ownership. (3) Recommend self-control systems, KPI-based monitoring, positive reinforcement. (4) Close: "too much control kills the very performance it tries to ensure."
CASELET 05 · STAFFING

Manpower Planning Calculation

"A company needs 80 production, 50 sales, 20 HR, 30 finance employees. Expects 10% expansion, 5% automation reduction." OR "1 employee handles 200 units; expected output 50,000 units; efficiency rises 10%." OR a Markov-style transition matrix.
DiagnosisNumerical staffing caselet — easy marks if you know the formula. Concepts to useIdentify the method (Bottom-up, Ratio, Regression, Markov, Delphi, Nominal Group). Structure(1) Name the method. (2) Show the formula explicitly. (3) Plug in numbers step by step. (4) State the final answer clearly. (5) One-line interpretation of what the number means for HR planning. Don't skip steps — partial credit lives in the working.
CASELET 06 · ORGANISING

Decentralisation vs Centralisation

"A growing company had a centralised model that worked pre-1991. Now with MNCs and market changes, even minor decisions sit at the top. CEO is overburdened, decisions are slow, market share is declining."
DiagnosisCentralisation hitting its limits in a changed environment. Concepts to useDecentralisation as a policy; the five importance points (relief to top management, develops initiative, develops managerial talent, quick decisions, better control); difference from delegation. Structure(1) Identify the problem as excessive centralisation. (2) Recommend decentralisation as the structural fix. (3) List 3-4 importance points. (4) Distinguish decentralisation from delegation (policy vs single act). (5) Cite the trigger — "in a fast-changing environment, decision speed at the periphery beats decision quality at the centre."
CASELET 07 · APPRAISAL

The Feedback Gap

"Employees are evaluated annually with no feedback during the year. At appraisal time, many are surprised by low ratings, conflicts rise. Management says 'we have a formal appraisal system.'"
DiagnosisOver-reliance on feedback control with no concurrent control. Concepts to useThree types of control (feedforward, concurrent, feedback); appraisal as developmental vs evaluative; link to motivation. Structure(1) Identify failure of concurrent control (no real-time feedback). (2) Explain why appraisal should be developmental, not just evaluative. (3) Recommend monthly/quarterly check-ins, informal coaching, feedforward guidance. (4) Close: "delayed feedback is as good as no feedback."
CASELET 08 · HRM

Hiring Bias / Signaling

"A company keeps hiring from top-tier institutes; performance variance is high. Some non-IIM hires outperform, but recruiters consistently shortlist by institute brand. HR director wants to redesign the process."
DiagnosisSignaling Theory in action with bias risk. Concepts to useInformation asymmetry; signals as imperfect proxies for productivity; bias risks; modern HR practices. Structure(1) Identify Signaling Theory and Spence's framework. (2) Explain why signals are useful (screening) but flawed (don't directly cause productivity). (3) Recommend combining signals with skill assessments, behavioural interviews, work simulations. (4) Mention employer branding as a counter-signal to attract talent beyond resume builders.
CASELET 09 · 7S FRAMEWORK

McKinsey 7S Misalignment

"A company has great strategy and skilled staff, but recently introduced a new top leader with an aggressive style. Shared values are eroding, systems haven't been updated, structure is unclear after restructuring. Performance has dropped."
DiagnosisMulti-S misalignment — strategy and skills are fine but style, systems, structure, and shared values are off. Concepts to useThe 7S framework; alignment over individual element strength; interdependence of hard and soft S's. Structure(1) Lay out all 7 S's. (2) Score each as strong/weak for this case. (3) Show how soft S misalignment is dragging hard S effectiveness. (4) Recommend culture and structure realignment before doubling down on strategy.
CASELET 10 · INTEGRATOR

Management in Action

"On paper, the company has perfect policies, structured hierarchy, defined processes. But employees report confusion, lack of motivation, blurred priorities, delayed feedback. The CEO is puzzled."
DiagnosisGap between formal system (written) and lived reality (executed). Concepts to useAll five functions in their "lived" form — daily planning clarity, real-time organising, integration in staffing, daily leadership behaviour, continuous control. Structure(1) Identify the formal-vs-lived gap as the core diagnosis. (2) Walk through each function showing how it should be lived day-to-day. (3) Recommend strengthening daily execution at the manager-employee interaction level. (4) Close: "management is what happens between people at work every single day, not what's written in handbooks."

Final Exam Tactics

  • Read all 5 caselets first. Pick the 4 you're most confident on. Don't burn time on one you don't recognise.
  • Concept identification = first line of every answer. "This case demonstrates [Concept] under the [Function] of management." Marker sees you got the diagnosis right immediately.
  • Use sub-headings and points, not paragraphs. Your prof literally said this.
  • Apply, don't just define. Every concept must be tied back to the case facts. "Ramesh is showing directive leadership failure because…"
  • Close each answer with a one-liner insight. Pick from the slide quotes — "people don't fail from incapacity, but from unclear expectations," "too much control kills the very performance it tries to ensure," "delayed feedback is as good as no feedback," "management is lived, not designed."
  • Time check at 1 hour. If you're more than halfway through caselet 2, you're slow. Speed up.
  • Don't leave any of the 4 blank. A weak answer beats a missing one. Even rough application earns marks.

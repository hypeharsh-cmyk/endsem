Chapter 10 Money, Interest and Income: The IS-LM Model

Book pp. 219–247 PDF pp. 237–265 Exam weight High Dornbusch · Fischer · Startz 11e

Chapter Highlights

  • The IS-LM model is the core of short-run macroeconomics. It simultaneously determines income $Y$ and the interest rate $i$.
  • The IS curve — goods market equilibrium — slopes downward: higher $i$ → lower investment → lower $Y$.
  • The LM curve — money market equilibrium — slopes upward: higher $Y$ → higher money demand → higher $i$.
  • Fiscal expansion shifts IS right → both $Y$ and $i$ rise. Higher $i$ crowds out investment — fiscal multiplier is smaller than the Ch. 9 multiplier $\alpha_G$.
  • Monetary expansion shifts LM right → $Y$ rises, $i$ falls.
  • The AD curve is derived by tracing IS-LM equilibria at different price levels: higher $P$ → lower $M/P$ → LM shifts left → lower $Y$.

1 · The IS Curve — Goods Market Equilibrium

Chapter 9 treated investment as entirely exogenous. Now we make it endogenous: investment depends on the interest rate.

1.1 Investment Demand Function

$$I = \bar{I} - b\,i \qquad b > 0$$
Equation (1) — Investment Demand
  • $\bar{I}$ = autonomous investment (independent of both $i$ and $Y$).
  • $b$ = interest-sensitivity of investment. Large $b$ → flatter IS. Small $b$ → steeper IS.
  • Higher $i$ raises borrowing costs → reduces profitability → firms invest less.

1.2 Deriving the IS Curve

Insert the investment function into the aggregate demand identity:

$$AD = \underbrace{\bigl[\bar{C} + c\,\overline{TR} + \bar{I} + G + \overline{NX}\bigr]}_{\bar{A}} + c(1-t)Y - bi$$
Equation (2) — Aggregate Demand with Interest Rate
IS Curve Derivation
1
Goods market equilibrium: $Y = AD = \bar{A} + c(1-t)Y - bi$
2
$Y - c(1-t)Y = \bar{A} - bi$ → $Y[1-c(1-t)] = \bar{A} - bi$
3
$$\boxed{Y = \alpha_G(\bar{A} - bi)}$$
where $\alpha_G = \dfrac{1}{1-c(1-t)}$ is the Ch. 9 multiplier. IS Equation (5).
4
Rearranging for $i$: $$i = \frac{\bar{A}}{b} - \frac{Y}{\alpha_G b} \quad \text{(IS as }i\text{ function of }Y)$$
Negative slope in $(Y, i)$ space — confirms IS is downward-sloping.
Fig 10.1 — IS Curve Derivation
Lower interest rate $i_2 < i_1$ increases investment → shifts AD up → higher equilibrium income $Y_2 > Y_1$. Plotting $(i,Y)$ pairs traces the downward-sloping IS curve.

1.3 Slope of the IS Curve

From the IS equation: $\Delta i / \Delta Y = -1/(\alpha_G b)$. The IS curve is steeper when:

1.4 IS Curve Shifts

The IS curve shifts horizontally by $\alpha_G \Delta\bar{A}$ when autonomous spending changes.

  • Rightward shift (IS→IS'): $\uparrow G$, $\uparrow\bar{I}$, $\uparrow\overline{TR}$, $\downarrow\overline{TA}$, $\uparrow\overline{NX}$, $\uparrow\bar{C}$
  • Leftward shift: opposite of above — contractionary fiscal policy, fall in investment confidence
Examiner Note
The IS shift is horizontal and equals $\alpha_G \Delta G$ (the Ch. 9 multiplier times the policy change). But the actual change in income in IS-LM is smaller than this shift, because the upward-sloping LM means the interest rate rises and crowds out some investment. This is the essential difference from Ch. 9.

2 · The LM Curve — Money Market Equilibrium

2.1 Demand for Real Balances

Definition

Real money demand (L) — demand for money expressed in purchasing power terms (nominal demand ÷ price level). People care about what money buys, not how many dollars they hold.

$$L = kY - hi \qquad k, h > 0$$
Equation (6) — Real Money Demand
  • $k$ = income sensitivity of money demand. Higher $Y$ → more transactions → more money needed.
  • $h$ = interest sensitivity of money demand. Higher $i$ → higher opportunity cost of holding money → people economise on cash.

2.2 Deriving the LM Curve

The central bank fixes the nominal money supply $\bar{M}$. With price level $\bar{P}$ constant, real money supply is $\bar{M}/\bar{P}$. Money market equilibrium: $L = \bar{M}/\bar{P}$.

LM Curve Derivation
1
Set $L = M/P$: $kY - hi = \bar{M}/\bar{P}$
2
Solve for $i$: $$\boxed{i = \frac{k}{h}Y - \frac{1}{h}\frac{\bar{M}}{\bar{P}}}$$
Equation (7a) — LM curve. Positive slope in $(Y,i)$ space.
3
Slope = $k/h > 0$: higher $Y$ → higher money demand → at fixed $M/P$, interest rate must rise to choke off excess demand.
Fig 10.2 — LM Curve Derivation
Higher income $Y_2 > Y_1$ shifts money demand right → at fixed $\bar{M}/\bar{P}$, equilibrium requires higher interest rate $i_2 > i_1$. Plotting these $(Y,i)$ pairs gives the upward-sloping LM.

3 · IS-LM Simultaneous Equilibrium

Definition

IS-LM equilibrium — the unique $(Y_0, i_0)$ that simultaneously clears both the goods market (IS) and the money market (LM).

Fig 10.3 — IS-LM Equilibrium
Point $E$ is the only combination of $(Y,i)$ where both markets clear simultaneously. Price level is held fixed; output is demand-determined.

3.1 Fiscal Expansion: IS Shifts Right

An increase in $G$ shifts IS right by $\alpha_G \Delta G$. The new equilibrium has higher $Y$ and higher $i$.

Fig 10.4 — Fiscal Expansion in IS-LM
IS shifts right by $\alpha_G \Delta G$. But the actual income increase $\Delta Y < \alpha_G \Delta G$ because the rising interest rate crowds out investment. The higher $i$ is the crowding-out mechanism.
Definition — Crowding Out

When fiscal expansion raises $i$, private investment falls: $\Delta I = -b\Delta i < 0$. Government spending partially displaces private investment. Crowding out is complete (fiscal policy is ineffective) only when the LM is vertical ($h = 0$, classical money demand).

Examiner Note — Why IS-LM Multiplier < Ch. 9 Multiplier
In Ch. 9, interest rates were fixed, so $\Delta Y = \alpha_G \Delta G$. In IS-LM, the rising $i$ from the fiscal expansion reduces investment, which partially offsets the multiplier. The correct IS-LM fiscal multiplier is $\gamma = h\alpha_G/(h + kb\alpha_G) < \alpha_G$. Examiners test whether you know this distinction — simply quoting $\alpha_G$ as the IS-LM multiplier is wrong.

3.2 Monetary Expansion: LM Shifts Right

An increase in $\bar{M}$ (or a decrease in $P$) raises the real money supply $\bar{M}/P$, shifting LM right. The new equilibrium has higher $Y$ and lower $i$.

Fig 10.5 — Monetary Expansion in IS-LM
LM shifts right: excess money supply lowers $i$ → investment rises → income rises. Monetary policy works through the interest rate channel: $\uparrow M \to \downarrow i \to \uparrow I \to \uparrow Y$.

4 · Deriving the AD Curve from IS-LM

The AD curve traces IS-LM equilibrium output at different price levels, holding $\bar{M}$ and $\bar{A}$ constant.

AD Derivation Logic
1
At price $P_1$: real money supply $= \bar{M}/P_1$. LM₁ gives equilibrium $(Y_1, i_1)$.
2
At higher price $P_2 > P_1$: real money supply $= \bar{M}/P_2 < \bar{M}/P_1$. LM₂ is to the left of LM₁.
3
LM₂ intersects IS at a lower income $Y_2 < Y_1$ and higher interest rate $i_2 > i_1$.
Plot $(P_2, Y_2)$ and $(P_1, Y_1)$: as $P \uparrow$, $Y$ falls → downward-sloping AD.
Fig 10.6 — Deriving the AD Curve from IS-LM
Top panel: IS-LM at two price levels. Higher $P$ → lower $M/P$ → LM shifts left → lower $Y$. Bottom panel: plotting $(P,Y)$ pairs traces the downward-sloping AD curve.
Examiner Note — AD Slope Mechanism
The AD curve slopes down through the real balance effect (also called the Keynes effect): $P\uparrow \Rightarrow M/P\downarrow \Rightarrow$ LM shifts left $\Rightarrow i\uparrow \Rightarrow I\downarrow \Rightarrow Y\downarrow$. This is the core of what Ch. 5 described as "higher prices reduce the real money supply and lower aggregate demand." Now you have the full IS-LM mechanism behind it.

5 · Formal IS-LM Algebra (Optional)

Substitute the LM equation (7a) into the IS equation (5) to solve for equilibrium $Y$ and $i$:

$$\boxed{Y_0 = \frac{h\,\alpha_G}{h + k\,b\,\alpha_G}\,\bar{A} + \frac{b\,\alpha_G}{h + k\,b\,\alpha_G}\,\frac{\bar{M}}{P}}$$
Equation (8) — Equilibrium Income (IS-LM)
$$i_0 = \frac{k\,\alpha_G}{h + k\,b\,\alpha_G}\,\bar{A} - \frac{\alpha_G}{h + k\,b\,\alpha_G}\,\frac{\bar{M}}{P}$$
Equation (9) — Equilibrium Interest Rate

5.1 IS-LM Policy Multipliers

$$\gamma_{\text{fiscal}} = \frac{\Delta Y}{\Delta G} = \frac{h\,\alpha_G}{h + k\,b\,\alpha_G} < \alpha_G$$
Fiscal multiplier in IS-LM — always less than Ch. 9 multiplier
$$\gamma_{\text{monetary}} = \frac{\Delta Y}{\Delta(M/P)} = \frac{b\,\alpha_G}{h + k\,b\,\alpha_G}$$
Monetary multiplier in IS-LM
Limiting CaseLM ShapeFiscal PolicyMonetary Policy
$h \to \infty$ (liquidity trap)Horizontal LMMaximum effectiveness: $\gamma_F = \alpha_G$Zero: $\gamma_M = 0$
$h \to 0$ (classical money demand)Vertical LMZero: $\gamma_F = 0$ (complete crowding out)Maximum effectiveness

Examiner Trap — Liquidity Trap
In the liquidity trap ($h \to \infty$, horizontal LM), the monetary multiplier is zero — printing money cannot lower interest rates or raise output. Fiscal policy is fully effective. This is the extreme Keynesian case and was used to argue for fiscal stimulus during the 2008-09 crisis with near-zero interest rates.

Revision — Key Distinctions

  • IS vs. LM slopes: IS negative (goods market); LM positive (money market). Different reasons for different slopes — never confuse them.
  • IS-LM multiplier vs. Ch. 9 multiplier: IS-LM fiscal multiplier $\gamma_F < \alpha_G$ due to interest rate rise (crowding out). This is the central result of Ch. 10.
  • Crowding out: fiscal expansion → $i\uparrow$ → $I\downarrow$. Complete only if LM is vertical (classical money demand).
  • Fiscal expansion: shifts IS right → $Y\uparrow$, $i\uparrow$. Monetary expansion: shifts LM right → $Y\uparrow$, $i\downarrow$. Sign of $\Delta i$ is the key distinction.
  • Deriving AD: Hold IS and $\bar{M}$ fixed; vary $P$ → LM shifts → different $Y$. AD slopes down via the real balance effect.
  • Liquidity trap: $h\to\infty$, horizontal LM → fiscal policy effective ($\gamma_F = \alpha_G$); monetary policy ineffective ($\gamma_M = 0$). Relevant at zero lower bound.
  • What shifts IS: any change in $\bar{A}$ — government spending, taxes, transfers, autonomous investment, consumer confidence, net exports.
  • What shifts LM: change in nominal money supply $\bar{M}$, or change in price level $P$ (which changes real balances $M/P$).

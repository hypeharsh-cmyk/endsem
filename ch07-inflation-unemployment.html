Overview

Chapter Highlights
  • Costs of unemployment: mainly forgone output — very large
  • Costs of anticipated inflation: small at moderate rates ("shoe-leather costs")
  • Costs of unanticipated inflation: large distributional effects — debtors win, creditors lose
  • Unemployment rates vary widely: by age, race, skill, country
  • The sacrifice ratio quantifies the output cost of disinflation
  • Hysteresis: prolonged high unemployment may raise the natural rate

Costs of Unemployment

Output Cost (Okun's Law)

Okun's Law
$$\frac{Y - Y^*}{Y^*} = -\omega(u - u^*), \quad \omega \approx 2$$

One percentage point of excess unemployment costs approximately 2% of GDP. A 3-point unemployment spike costs 6% of GDP — hundreds of billions of dollars.

The costs of unemployment fall disproportionately on:

The Weighted Average Formula
$$u = w_1 u_1 + w_2 u_2 + \cdots + w_n u_n$$

Overall unemployment is a weighted average of group rates. The aggregate rate can rise even if each group's rate is unchanged — if high-unemployment groups (e.g., teenagers) grow as a share of the labour force.

Costs of Inflation

Anticipated Inflation

Small at Moderate Rates
  • Shoe-leather costs: people hold less cash → more trips to bank (implicit cost of inflation tax)
  • Menu costs: costs of updating price lists, renegotiating contracts
  • Distorted tax brackets: bracket creep if tax system not fully indexed
  • At single-digit rates, these costs are minor relative to the costs of unemployment

Unanticipated Inflation

Hyperinflation

When Money Breaks Down

At very high inflation (prices doubling monthly), money ceases to function as a medium of exchange. Barter replaces monetary exchange, transaction costs explode, and output can drop dramatically. Historical examples: Weimar Germany (1923), Zimbabwe (2008), Venezuela (ongoing).

Examiner Insight: The exam frequently asks you to distinguish anticipated vs unanticipated inflation costs. The key: anticipated inflation allows parties to prepare (index contracts, adjust nominal rates); unanticipated creates arbitrary wealth redistributions.

The Sacrifice Ratio

Definition

The sacrifice ratio is the cumulative percentage of GDP lost for each 1 percentage point permanent reduction in the inflation rate.

$$\text{Sacrifice Ratio} = \frac{\sum\text{Output losses}}{\Delta\pi} \approx 2\text{–}4\%$$

Deriving the Sacrifice Ratio

Using the Phillips curve slope ($\varepsilon \approx 0.5$) and Okun's Law ($\omega \approx 2$):

StepEquationResult
1 point of $\pi$ reduction$\Delta\pi = -\varepsilon \cdot \Delta u = -0.5 \cdot \Delta u$Requires $\Delta u = +2$ percentage points
2 points of excess unemployment$\Delta Y/Y^* = -\omega \cdot \Delta u = -2 \times 2$Output loss = 4% of GDP
Implied sacrifice ratio~4% of GDP per point of disinflation (rough)
Why Ball's Estimates Are Lower (~2.39% for US)

During actual disinflations, expectations fall too. The drop in $\pi^e$ causes the short-run Phillips curve to shift down, providing extra disinflation on top of the movement along the curve. This additional effect reduces the sacrifice ratio below the rough 4% estimate.

Policy Implication: The sacrifice ratio shows that disinflation is very costly. The Volcker disinflation (1980–83) brought US inflation from ~10% to ~3% — but at the cost of unemployment rising to nearly 11% and GDP losses in the trillions.

Anatomy of Unemployment

Five key characteristics of US labour market unemployment:

Five Stylised Facts
  1. Large variation across groups (age, race, experience)
  2. High turnover: flows into/out of employment are large relative to stocks
  3. Cyclical pattern: layoffs spike in recessions, quits fall
  4. Most unemployment spells are short in duration
  5. Most unemployment is accounted for by workers in long spells — the long-term unemployed
Note the paradox in points 4 & 5: Most individual spells are short, but at any given moment, the pool is dominated by people in long spells. This is like a hospital — most patients are discharged quickly, but hospital beds are occupied mostly by the seriously ill.

At any time there is a pool of unemployed people with flows in and out:

During recessions: hours per worker cut first, then layoffs increase, quits fall; prolonged recession creates discouraged workers (undercount of unemployment).

Cyclical vs Frictional Unemployment

TypeDefinitionWhen presentPolicy response
Frictional (= natural rate $u^*$) Unemployment even at full employment due to normal search, matching, and turnover Always present; irreducible minimum Structural: job training, placement services
Cyclical ($u - u^*$) Unemployment in excess of the natural rate; occurs when $Y < Y^*$ During downturns and recessions Aggregate demand policy (fiscal/monetary)
The Unemployment Rate Identity
$$u = u^* + (u - u^*) = \text{frictional} + \text{cyclical}$$

The Natural Rate of Unemployment (NAIRU)

Definition

The NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) is the unemployment rate at which inflation is neither accelerating nor decelerating — i.e., where $\pi = \pi^e$.

$$u^* = w_1 u_1^* + w_2 u_2^* + \cdots + w_n u_n^*$$

Historical NAIRU Estimates (US)

Important Caveat: The NAIRU is not a fixed constant — "not an intertemporal constant, something like the speed of light" (Phelps). It shifts with the demographic composition of the labour force, unemployment benefits, search technology, and labour market institutions.

Search Unemployment

Voluntary Search

Workers may voluntarily leave jobs to search for better ones. This is rational: if job quality varies, waiting for a good match is worthwhile. Higher benefits extend search duration. Higher wages at the margin justify longer search. Search unemployment is part of frictional unemployment.

Hysteresis and the Rising Natural Rate

Unemployment Hysteresis

Hysteresis is the phenomenon where prolonged high unemployment raises the natural rate itself — the natural rate depends on the history of actual unemployment.

Mechanisms of Hysteresis

MechanismHow it raises u*
Skill depreciationLong-term unemployed lose skills; productivity falls; less attractive to hire
DiscouragementUnemployed learn to live on benefits; reduce effort in job search
Employer signallingLong unemployment spells signal low quality to employers → self-reinforcing exclusion
Insider-outsider dynamicsInsiders set wages excluding outsiders; high wages persist even with unemployment
Fig 7-3 — Hysteresis: Extended Recession Raises u*
European Evidence

European unemployment averaged ~4% in the 1970s, nearly 10% in the 1980s. Hysteresis theorists argue $u^*$ rose dramatically — partly because Europe's strong employment protection laws meant firms hired less, and workers excluded during the 1970s oil shocks were permanently marginalised.

Disinflation

Disinflation is the process of reducing the rate of inflation (not to be confused with deflation = negative inflation).

The Disinflation Process in IS-LM / PC Terms

StepWhat happensEffect on $u$ and $\pi$
1Central bank tightens money → LM shifts left$Y$ falls; $u$ rises above $u^*$
2$u > u^*$ → $\pi < \pi^e$ (move right along PC)Inflation falls; but expected inflation sticky
3Sustained excess $u$ → $\pi^e$ gradually fallsPC shifts down; less inflation at any $u$
4Eventually $\pi^e = \pi_{\text{target}}$; economy at $u^*$ at new lower $\pi$Long-run: lower $\pi$, same $u^*$
Volcker Disinflation (1980–83)

Fed raised the federal funds rate sharply → severe recession → $u$ peaked at 10.8% (late 1982) → inflation fell from ~10% to ~3%. Estimated sacrifice ratio: ~2.5 to 3% per point of inflation. Long-term benefit: inflation expectations anchored low for decades.

Cold Turkey vs Gradual Disinflation
StrategyInflation fallOutput costRational expectations prediction
Cold turkey (credible, fast)FastLower (if credible)$\pi^e$ falls fast → less sacrifice
GradualismSlowSpread over time$\pi^e$ falls slowly; same total cost

The Political Business Cycle

Theory

Incumbent politicians manipulate economic policy to create good conditions at election time: expansion before elections (lower $u$, higher $Y$) at the cost of higher inflation or austerity afterwards.

PhasePolicy actionEconomic outcomeElectoral logic
Pre-election (2 years)Expansionary: ↑G, ↓taxes, loose money↓$u$, ↑$Y$, ↑$\pi$ (lagged)Voters reward low unemployment
Post-electionContractionary: tighten to fight inflation↑$u$, ↓$\pi$Voters forget — next cycle begins
Criticism: Rational voters should anticipate the cycle and discount it. The theory works better when voters have incomplete information about the economy's true state (informational asymmetry between government and public).

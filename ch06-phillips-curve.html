The Original Phillips Curve

In 1958, A. W. Phillips studied UK wage data for 1861–1957. Key finding: an inverse relationship between unemployment and the rate of wage inflation.

Simple Phillips Curve Equation

Definition

Rate of wage inflation:

$$g_w = \frac{W_{t+1} - W_t}{W_t}$$

Simple Phillips curve:

$$g_w = -\varepsilon(u - u^*)$$

where $\varepsilon > 0$ measures wage sensitivity to unemployment, $u^*$ = natural rate (NAIRU)

Interpretation

ConditionResultIntuition
$u > u^*$$g_w < 0$, wages fallingLabour market slack; workers accept lower wages
$u = u^*$$g_w = 0$, wages stableFull employment — no upward or downward pressure
$u < u^*$$g_w > 0$, wages risingLabour market tight; workers bargain for higher wages

Equivalently, wages next period:

$$W_{t+1} = W_t[1 - \varepsilon(u - u^*)]$$
Fig 6-1 — Original Phillips Curve (Downward Sloping)
Policy Tradeoff (1960s View)

Policymakers thought they faced a permanent menu of choices along the Phillips curve: accept higher inflation for lower unemployment, or accept higher unemployment to keep inflation low. The 1960s US data appeared to confirm this.

The Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve

The simple Phillips curve collapsed in the 1970s as stagflation appeared. The missing ingredient: expected inflation.

Intuition

Workers and firms negotiate over real wages. If inflation is expected to be 10%, a 3% nominal raise is actually a 7% real wage cut. What matters is excess of wage growth over expected inflation.

Deriving the Modern Phillips Curve

1
Original wage-inflation PC:
$$g_w = -\varepsilon(u - u^*)$$
Workers care about wage growth relative to expected inflation
2
Augment with expected inflation $\pi^e$:
$$(g_w - \pi^e) = -\varepsilon(u - u^*)$$
Excess of wage inflation over expected inflation drives unemployment effects
3
Assuming constant real wage: $\pi = g_w$, so:
$$\boxed{\pi = \pi^e - \varepsilon(u - u^*)}$$
Modern (expectations-augmented) Phillips curve — the workhorse of AS theory
Fig 6-2 — Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve (Short-Run Shifts with πe)
Two Critical Properties
  • Expected inflation passes one-for-one into actual inflation: if $\pi^e$ rises by 1%, the whole short-run PC shifts up by 1%
  • At $u = u^*$: $\pi = \pi^e$ — unemployment is at the natural rate when actual inflation equals expected inflation

Adaptive Expectations: Testing the Model

Assuming people expect this year's inflation to equal last year's: $\pi^e_t = \pi_{t-1}$, the Phillips curve becomes:

$$\pi_t - \pi_{t-1} = -\varepsilon(u - u^*)$$

Empirical slope estimate: $\varepsilon \approx 0.5$ — one extra point of unemployment reduces inflation by only about half a point. This confirms the short-run Phillips curve is quite flat.

Stagflation

Definition

Stagflation = simultaneous high unemployment (stagnation) and high inflation. Example: 1982, US had 9% unemployment and 6% inflation.

How Stagflation Occurs

1
Economy experienced high inflation in the 1970s → $\pi^e$ rises to ~7%
Short-run Phillips curve shifts up — now intersects $u^*$ at 7% inflation
2
Recession hits → $u > u^*$ → $\pi$ falls, but only partially
Move right along the high-$\pi^e$ curve — $\pi$ falls below $\pi^e$ but stays well above zero
3
Result: high unemployment AND high inflation simultaneously
Cannot be explained by simple Phillips curve; requires expectations
Fig 6-3 — Stagflation: High πe + Recession
Examiner Note: Stagflation is not a mystery once you understand expectations. It is simply a recession on a short-run Phillips curve that has shifted up due to prior high inflation. The 1982 situation (point S in Fig 6-3) is the canonical exam example.

Rational Expectations

The Rational Expectations Revolution

Robert Lucas (1973): if people form expectations rationally (using all available information), then predictable changes in monetary policy should not affect output — only surprise changes can move $u$ away from $u^*$.

The Logic

The modern Phillips curve: $\pi = \pi^e - \varepsilon(u - u^*)$

If agents rationally set $\pi^e = E[\pi]$ = model's predicted $\pi$, then substituting back implies $u = u^*$ always. Systematic monetary policy just changes $\pi^e$ instantly, leaving output unchanged.

Rational vs. Adaptive Expectations
AspectAdaptive expectationsRational expectations
How $\pi^e$ is formed$\pi^e_t = \pi_{t-1}$ (backward-looking)$\pi^e_t = E_t[\pi_t]$ (model-consistent)
PC shifts in response to policyGradually (only after inflation is observed)Immediately (when policy announced)
Effect of announced ΔMReal effects for extended periodOnly $\pi$ changes, $u$ unchanged
ImplicationSlow adjustment; prolonged recessionsPolicy ineffective unless surprising

Why Rational Expectations Doesn't Fully Explain the World

  • Long-term contracts: wages fixed for 2–3 years; cannot adjust even if expectations are rational
  • Slow learning: even rational agents need time to update models
  • Menu costs: cost of changing prices can outweigh benefit of getting price exactly right
  • Empirical evidence: monetary policy demonstrably has real effects for significant periods

Why Are Wages Sticky?

The Phillips curve requires slow wage adjustment. Multiple theories explain why nominal wages adjust sluggishly:

TheoryMechanismImplication
Imperfect Information
(Friedman-Phelps)		 Workers mistake nominal for real wage increases; supply more labour temporarily Short-run AS upward sloping; disappears when expectations correct
Coordination failure No firm raises price first (loses business); no firm cuts wage first (loses workers) Prices & wages move slowly even without individual irrationality
Efficiency wages Wages above market-clearing motivate workers; firms won't cut Wages are downward rigid; unemployment can persist
Insider-outsider
(Lindbeck-Snower)		 Employed insiders bargain wages; unemployed outsiders excluded from table Wages don't fall much with unemployment; recovery is slow
Long-term contracts Wages set for 2–3 years; staggered across firms and industries Aggregate wage adjustment slow even if each contract is rational

Contracts & Staggered Wage Setting

The wage-employment relationship (derived from PC):

$$g_w = \pi^e + \frac{W_{t+1} - W_t}{W_t} = \pi^e - \varepsilon\frac{N^* - N}{N^*} \tag{WN}$$

The January–July Example

How Staggering Slows Adjustment
  • Half the workforce renegotiates in January; half in July
  • Money stock rises in September
  • No wage adjustment until January (3-month delay minimum)
  • January workers don't fully adjust because July wages won't change for 6 more months — over-adjusting would price themselves out of jobs
  • July workers similarly can't fully adjust because January wages are already fixed
  • Wages leapfrog each other, rising only partway toward equilibrium each period
  • Full adjustment takes multiple rounds of renegotiation
Key Insight: Staggered contracts explain why the aggregate supply curve shifts up gradually even if every individual contract is perfectly rational. The position of the AS curve in any period depends on where it was last period.

From Phillips Curve to Aggregate Supply Curve

Four-step derivation linking employment, wages, prices, and output.

1
Okun's Law: translate output gap to unemployment gap
$$\frac{Y - Y^*}{Y^*} = -\omega(u - u^*), \quad \omega \approx 2$$
1 extra point of unemployment costs about 2% of GDP
2
Price–cost markup: firms price at markup over unit labour cost
$$P = (1+z)\frac{W}{a}$$
$z$ = markup, $a$ = labour productivity (output per worker), $W/a$ = unit labour cost
3
Phillips curve: wage growth depends on employment gap and expected inflation
$$g_w = \pi^e - \varepsilon\frac{N^* - N}{N^*}$$
This drives price inflation because $P \propto W$ (from markup equation)
4
Combining all three → AS curve:
$$P_{t+1} = P^e_{t+1}\left[1 + \lambda\left(\frac{Y - Y^*}{Y^*}\right)\right]$$
Aggregate supply curve: next period's price rises with expected price and output gap
Fig 6-4 — Aggregate Supply Curve with Shifting PC
Key Property of the AS Curve

The AS curve shifts over time as expected inflation changes:

  • $Y > Y^*$ this period → $P^e$ rises next period → AS shifts up
  • $Y < Y^*$ this period → $P^e$ falls next period → AS shifts down
  • Long-run AS is vertical at $Y^*$ (automatic adjustment mechanism)

Okun's Law

Definition
$$\frac{Y - Y^*}{Y^*} = -\omega(u - u^*), \quad \omega \approx 2$$

One extra percentage point of unemployment is associated with approximately 2 percentage points of lost GDP relative to potential.

$u - u^*$Output gap $(Y-Y^*)/Y^*$Interpretation
+1%-2%1 point above NAIRU → lose 2% of GDP
00Unemployment at natural rate; output at potential
-1%+2%1 point below NAIRU → output 2% above potential (overheating)
Why ω ≈ 2 not 1? When unemployment falls 1%, firms hire workers who were marginally employed before, work longer hours, and productivity rises cyclically — so output increases by more than 1%.

Supply Shocks

Definition

A supply shock is a disturbance whose first impact is a shift in the AS curve, not the AD curve.

Adverse Supply Shock (e.g., OPEC Oil Embargo 1973)

1
Oil price spike → unit production costs rise → firms supply less at every price
AS curve shifts up: same output now requires higher price
2
Economy moves from $E$ to $E'$: $P$ rises, $Y$ falls
Double blow: higher prices AND lower output — the supply-shock recession
3
Without policy response: $Y < Y^*$ → wages gradually fall → AS shifts back down → slow recovery to $E$
Real wage falls as nominal wage is dragged down by unemployment
Fig 6-5 — Adverse Supply Shock: AS Shifts Up

Accommodating vs. Non-Accommodating Policy

Policy responseAD moves toOutputPrice levelReal wage
No accommodation (passive)Stays at AD↓ to $Y'$↑ to $P'$Eventually ↓ as wages fall
Accommodate (expand AD)AD shifts to AD'Stays at $Y^*$↑ fully (more inflation)↓ immediately via higher P
The Accommodation Dilemma

Accommodating policy avoids the output recession but validates the full price increase. Non-accommodating policy causes a recession but eventually returns the economy to the original price level. Policymakers face a tradeoff: accept more inflation now, or more unemployment now.

Accommodating Supply Shocks

After an adverse shock, AD can be expanded to maintain $Y^*$, but at the cost of higher prices:

$$P^* > P' > P \quad \text{(fully accommodated price rise)}$$

The requirement for accommodation: the real wage must fall. Accommodation achieves the real wage cut via higher prices rather than lower nominal wages — a faster but more inflationary path.

Historical Context: 1973 OPEC shock — neither fiscal nor monetary policy responded. Unemployment hit 8%+ by late 1974. Then in 1975–76, policy turned stimulatory, accelerating the recovery. The 1979 shock was accommodated less, leading to the 1980–82 recession but ultimately breaking the inflation spiral.

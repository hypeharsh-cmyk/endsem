Overview

Chapter Highlights
  • Both fiscal and monetary policy shift the IS-LM equilibrium and stabilise the economy
  • Crowding out: fiscal expansion → higher $i$ → lower private investment → partially offsets the expansion
  • Liquidity trap (horizontal LM): fiscal policy maximally effective; monetary policy powerless
  • Classical case (vertical LM): monetary policy maximally effective; fiscal policy has no effect
  • Policy mix determines the composition of output, not just the level

The IS-LM model from Chapter 10 is here used to derive policy multipliers and to analyse real-world episodes including the 2008–09 Great Recession, when the Fed drove the federal funds rate to the zero lower bound.

Monetary Policy

In the US the Federal Reserve conducts monetary policy mainly through open market operations: buying bonds injects money (LM shifts right); selling bonds withdraws money (LM shifts left).

Mechanism of an Open-Market Purchase

1
Fed buys bonds → nominal $M$ rises → real money supply $\overline{M}/P$ rises
Price level is fixed in the short run, so real balances increase proportionally
2
Excess supply of money → public buys bonds → bond prices rise → yields (interest rates) fall
Money and asset markets adjust rapidly — immediate jump to LM' in the diagram
3
Lower $i$ → investment spending rises → aggregate demand rises → output expands
Economy slides up along LM'; rising $Y$ raises money demand, pushing $i$ back up slightly
4
New equilibrium $E'$: $Y' > Y_0$, $i' < i_0$
Both goods and money markets clear simultaneously

Monetary Policy Multiplier

From IS-LM algebra:

$$\frac{\Delta Y}{\Delta(M/P)} = \frac{b \alpha_G}{h + kb\alpha_G}$$

Larger effect when: $h$ small (steep LM), $b$ large (flat IS), $\alpha_G$ large (big multiplier), $k$ small (low income sensitivity of money demand)

If either link breaks, monetary policy loses its effectiveness:

  • Link 1 breaks: portfolio imbalances don't change interest rates (liquidity trap)
  • Link 2 breaks: spending doesn't respond to interest rates (inelastic investment)
Examiner Insight: The 1991 credit crunch illustrates a broken link 2 — banks refused to lend despite lower rates, severing the transmission from Fed policy to investment spending.

The Liquidity Trap

Definition

A situation where the public is willing to hold any amount of money at the prevailing interest rate — the LM curve is horizontal. Money supply changes cannot alter the interest rate.

Conditions and Implications

Real-World Episodes

Japan 1990s–2000s

Interest rates fell to ~0.5% and then effectively zero. Conventional monetary policy exhausted. Fiscal policy became the dominant stabilisation tool.

United States 2008–09

Fed drove the federal funds rate from 5% (Aug 2007) → 0.16% (Dec 2008). Conventional policy exhausted → Fed turned to quantitative easing: buying long-term Treasuries, agency debt, and mortgage-backed securities to reduce long-term rates.

Zero Lower Bound Logic

Why the Nominal Rate Can't Go Below Zero

If $i < 0$: borrow $100 today, keep as cash, repay $95 in a year — riskless profit. This implies infinite money demand, preventing negative nominal rates. Deflation pushes the real economy toward this bound.

$$i_{\text{nominal}} = r + \pi^e \quad \xrightarrow{\pi^e < 0} \quad i_{\text{nom}} \to 0$$

The Classical Case

Definition

The LM curve is vertical when the demand for money is completely insensitive to the interest rate ($h = 0$). The money market equation collapses to:

$$\frac{\bar{M}}{P} = kY \quad\Rightarrow\quad M = k \cdot PY$$

This is the classical quantity theory of money: nominal GDP ($PY$) depends only on the quantity of money.

Fig 11-3 — Classical Case: Vertical LM

Policy Implications — Classical Case

PolicyEffect on $Y$Effect on $i$Reason
Monetary expansionMaximum ↑↓LM shifts right; unique $Y$ determined by $M$
Fiscal expansion0↑↑$Y$ fixed by $M/P$; IS shift only raises $i$
Full Crowding Out: With vertical LM, $\Delta G$ raises $i$ until $\Delta I = -\Delta G$ exactly. Government spending displaces private investment dollar-for-dollar. Output is unchanged.

Fiscal Policy & Crowding Out

The IS curve equation (from Ch. 10):

$$Y = \alpha_G(\bar{A} - bi), \quad \alpha_G = \frac{1}{1-c(1-t)}$$

Government spending $G$ and transfers $TR$ enter through autonomous spending $\bar{A}$; the tax rate $t$ enters through the multiplier $\alpha_G$.

Effect of an Increase in Government Spending

1
↑$G$ → autonomous spending $\bar{A}$ rises → IS shifts right by $\alpha_G \Delta G$
Horizontal distance of the shift equals the goods-market multiplier times $\Delta G$
2
At fixed $i$, economy moves to $E'$: $\Delta Y = \alpha_G \Delta G$ (Chapter 9 result)
But the money market is now out of equilibrium — excess demand for money
3
Excess money demand → $i$ rises → investment spending falls (crowding out)
Firms reduce capital expenditure as borrowing becomes more expensive
4
New equilibrium $E''$: $Y'' < Y'$ and $i'' > i_0$
IS and LM intersect at a higher $i$ and lower $Y$ than the naive multiplier predicts
Crowding Out — Definition

The rise in the interest rate that accompanies a fiscal expansion crowds out private investment spending. Output rises by less than $\alpha_G \Delta G$.

$$\Delta Y_{\text{actual}} = \gamma_F \cdot \Delta G, \quad \gamma_F = \frac{h \alpha_G}{h + kb\alpha_G} < \alpha_G$$

Monetary Accommodation

Monetary accommodation (also called monetising the deficit) occurs when the central bank expands the money supply during a fiscal expansion to prevent interest rates from rising.

Mechanism

IS shifts right (fiscal expansion) AND LM shifts right simultaneously (Fed buys bonds to prevent $i$ rising).

$$\text{Result: } Y \uparrow\uparrow, \quad i \approx i_0 \text{ (unchanged or rises less)}$$
Fig 11-6 — Monetary Accommodation of Fiscal Expansion

Conditions for Accommodation

  • Central bank must be willing to expand $M$ in response to government deficits
  • Removes crowding out entirely — investment need not fall
  • Risk: if economy is at full employment, accommodation causes inflation (supply-side constraint)
Examiner Trap: Accommodation eliminates the rise in $i$, so investment is NOT crowded out. This means $\Delta Y$ is larger than with unaccommodated fiscal policy but the composition of output is different (more government, more consumption — but also sustained investment).

Composition of Output & Policy Effects

Different policies reach the same $Y$ but via different output compositions — a crucial insight for the policy mix debate.

Key Insight

Monetary expansion is the only policy that simultaneously raises output and private investment. Fiscal expansion (without accommodation) always crowds out some investment.

Investment Subsidy

An investment tax credit directly subsidises capital spending. The investment function shifts:

$$I = \bar{I} - bi \quad\longrightarrow\quad I = \bar{I} + \Delta I - bi$$
1
Subsidy shifts autonomous investment up by $\Delta I$
At every interest rate, firms invest more
2
IS shifts right by $\alpha_G \Delta I$
Same mechanism as a government spending increase
3
$i$ rises (due to LM constraint), but $I$ is still higher net
Unlike regular G increase, investment goes UP despite higher $i$ — subsidy effect dominates
4
Result: $Y \uparrow$, $i \uparrow$, $I \uparrow$, $C \uparrow$ (induced by higher $Y$)
Both consumption AND investment rise — unlike tax cut or G increase
Unique Feature of Investment Subsidy: It is the only fiscal policy where investment rises even though interest rates go up. All other fiscal expansions reduce investment (crowding out).

The Policy Mix

To reach full-employment output $Y^*$ from a recessionary equilibrium $E$, policymakers can use different combinations of monetary and fiscal policy.

Political Economy of the Policy Mix
  • Conservatives: tax cuts + spending cuts → shrink government over cycles
  • Progressives: expand government spending on education, environment, job training
  • Growth advocates: low interest rates / investment subsidies → capital accumulation

Policy Mix in Historical Context

1981–83: Tight Money + Loose Fiscal Policy

The 1980s Policy Mix

Reagan's tax cuts expanded the deficit (IS shifted right). Fed under Volcker tightened money aggressively to break inflation (LM shifted left). Result: very high real interest rates, severe recession in 1981–82, then rapid recovery as inflation fell. Investment and net exports were heavily crowded out.

2008–09: Zero Lower Bound & Fiscal Stimulus

Great Recession Policy Mix

Fed drove federal funds rate from 5% (Aug 2007) to 0.16% (Dec 2008). Once at zero lower bound, conventional monetary policy exhausted. Congress enacted the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (2009): ~$787 billion stimulus. Fed used quantitative easing as unorthodox substitute for rate cuts.

$$i \to 0 \Rightarrow \text{monetary policy impotent} \Rightarrow \text{fiscal policy maximally effective (liquidity trap logic)}$$

Germany 1990s: Reunification Shock

German Reunification

Massive fiscal transfers to East Germany → IS shifted right sharply. Bundesbank tightened money to fight inflation → LM shifted left. Result: very high European interest rates, contributing to the 1992 ERM crisis and recessions in UK and other European countries.

Exam Application: The 1991 recession shows declining output AND declining interest rates — consistent with a leftward IS shift (demand shock), NOT a monetary contraction (which would shift LM left and raise rates).

Chapter 9 Income, Spending and the Multiplier

Book pp. 194–218 PDF pp. 212–236 Exam weight High Dornbusch · Fischer · Startz 11e

Chapter Highlights

  • In the Keynesian model, spending determines output and output determines income, which then determines spending — a circular feedback.
  • Increases in autonomous spending raise output more than one-for-one: the multiplier effect.
  • The multiplier $\alpha = \dfrac{1}{1-c}$ depends on the MPC. Higher MPC → larger multiplier.
  • Income taxes reduce the multiplier and act as automatic stabilisers.
  • Increased government spending raises the deficit — tax revenues rise by less than spending because the multiplier is finite.

1 · Aggregate Demand and Equilibrium Output

Definition

Aggregate Demand (AD) — the total amount of goods demanded in the economy, decomposed as:

$$AD = C + I + G + NX$$
Definition

Equilibrium output — the level at which quantity produced equals quantity demanded:

$$Y = AD \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad I_U = Y - AD = 0$$

where $I_U$ is unplanned inventory investment. If $I_U > 0$ (excess production), firms cut back. If $I_U < 0$ (excess demand), firms expand. The system self-corrects to $Y = AD$.

This chapter assumes prices are fixed (horizontal Keynesian AS) and focuses entirely on what determines the position of the AD schedule. Interest rates are introduced in Chapter 10 (IS-LM).

2 · The Consumption Function

$$C = \bar{C} + cY \qquad \bar{C} > 0,\quad 0 < c < 1$$
Equation (4) — Consumption Function
  • $\bar{C}$ = autonomous consumption (consumption when $Y = 0$; reflects wealth, expectations).
  • $c$ = slope of the consumption function = MPC.
  • Empirical US estimate (Dornbusch): $C = -1354 + 0.97\,Y_D$ — an MPC of ~0.97 out of disposable income.

2.1 MPC and MPS

Definition

Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC) $= c = \Delta C / \Delta Y$. The fraction of each extra dollar of income devoted to consumption. $0 < c < 1$.

Definition

Marginal Propensity to Save (MPS) $= s = 1 - c = \Delta S / \Delta Y$. Since income is either consumed or saved, MPC + MPS $= 1$ by definition.

Example: c = 0.9

MPC = 0.9 → of each extra dollar of income, 90¢ is consumed, 10¢ is saved (MPS = 0.1).

Multiplier $= 1/(1-0.9) = 10$.

Example: c = 0.8

MPC = 0.8 → 80¢ consumed, 20¢ saved (MPS = 0.2).

Multiplier $= 1/(1-0.8) = 5$.

2.2 Savings Function

Since $S = Y - C$ (budget constraint), substituting the consumption function:

$$S = -\bar{C} + (1-c)Y = -\bar{C} + sY$$
Equation (6) — Savings Function

Saving is an increasing function of income. Note the symmetry: any theory of consumption is simultaneously a theory of saving.

3 · Autonomous Spending and Aggregate Demand

Adding government ($G$, $TA = \bar{TA}$, $TR = \overline{TR}$) and net exports ($\overline{NX}$), all assumed autonomous (exogenous) for now. Disposable income:

$$Y_D = Y + TR - TA$$

Consumption now depends on $Y_D$:

$$C = \bar{C} + c(Y + \overline{TR} - \overline{TA})$$

Aggregate demand becomes:

$$AD = \underbrace{\bigl[\bar{C} + c(\overline{TR} - \overline{TA}) + \bar{I} + G + \overline{NX}\bigr]}_{\bar{A}\,=\,\text{autonomous spending}} + cY$$
Equation (9) — Aggregate Demand Function
Fig 9.1 — 45° Diagram: Equilibrium Income
The AD schedule ($AD = \bar{A} + cY$) intersects the 45° line ($AD = Y$) at equilibrium $E$. Below $Y_0$: $AD > Y$ → inventories fall → firms expand. Above $Y_0$: $Y > AD$ → inventories pile up → firms cut back.

4 · Equilibrium Income

Algebraic Derivation of Equilibrium Income
1
Equilibrium condition: $Y = AD = \bar{A} + cY$
2
Collect $Y$ terms: $Y - cY = \bar{A}$ $\;\Rightarrow\;$ $Y(1-c) = \bar{A}$
3
$$Y_0 = \frac{1}{1-c}\,\bar{A} = \alpha\,\bar{A}$$
Equation (12). $\alpha = 1/(1-c)$ is the multiplier. Higher $c$ or higher $\bar{A}$ → higher equilibrium $Y$.

4.1 Saving = Investment (Alternative Equilibrium Condition)

In the simplest model (no government, no trade), $Y = C + S$ and $Y = C + I$. Therefore:

$$S = I \quad \text{(equilibrium condition)}$$

With government and trade, this generalises to:

$$I = S + \underbrace{(TA - TR - G)}_{\text{govt. surplus}} - \underbrace{NX}_{\text{net exports}}$$
Equation (14) — Saving-Investment Identity
Examiner Note
The $S = I$ equilibrium condition appears in 2-mark questions constantly. Examiners expect you to derive it from national income accounting, not just state it. Write: $Y = C + I$ and $Y = C + S$, so $S = I$. In the general case include government surplus and net imports.

5 · The Multiplier

Definition

Multiplier $(\alpha)$ — the amount by which equilibrium output changes when autonomous spending rises by 1 unit:

$$\alpha = \frac{\Delta Y_0}{\Delta \bar{A}} = \frac{1}{1-c}$$
Equation (17)

Why is the multiplier greater than 1? A $1 increase in autonomous spending raises income by $1. Recipients spend $c$ of that, raising income again. And so on.

5.1 Geometric Series Derivation

Derivation via Geometric Series (Eq. 15)
1
Round 1: autonomous spending rises by $\Delta\bar{A}$. Production rises by $\Delta\bar{A}$ to meet demand.
2
Round 2: that $\Delta\bar{A}$ of extra income induces $c\Delta\bar{A}$ of extra consumption.
Production rises by $c\Delta\bar{A}$.
3
Round 3: extra income of $c\Delta\bar{A}$ induces $c^2\Delta\bar{A}$ of consumption. And so on.
4
Total change in output: $$\Delta Y = \Delta\bar{A}(1 + c + c^2 + c^3 + \cdots) = \frac{\Delta\bar{A}}{1-c} = \alpha\,\Delta\bar{A}$$
Valid because $0 < c < 1$, so the geometric series converges.

5.2 Graphical Interpretation

Fig 9.2 — The Multiplier: Parallel AD Shift
AD shifts up by $\Delta\bar{A}$ (a parallel shift). Equilibrium moves from $E$ to $E'$. The income increase $\Delta Y_0 = \alpha\,\Delta\bar{A}$ is larger than the original spending increase. The gap between the two equilibria along the 45° line measures the multiplier effect.
Common Mistake
Do not say "a $\$1$ increase in government spending raises income by $\$1$." That misses the multiplier. The correct statement is $\Delta Y = \alpha \cdot \Delta G = \dfrac{1}{1-c}\Delta G$. For $c = 0.8$, a $\$1$ increase in $G$ raises $Y$ by $\$5$.
Examiner Note — Real-World Multiplier
Dornbusch cites Robert Hall's empirical estimate of the US fiscal multiplier at approximately 1.7 (Brookings, 2009), well below the simple formula's prediction. This is because the simple model ignores interest rate effects (crowding out) and import leakages. Examiners in open-economy questions expect you to note that $\alpha < 1/(1-c)$ in practice.

6 · The Government Sector

Government introduces three instruments: purchases $G$, transfers $\overline{TR}$, and a proportional income tax at rate $t$ (so $TA = tY$). Disposable income becomes $Y_D = Y + \overline{TR} - tY = \overline{TR} + (1-t)Y$.

Consumption with taxes:

$$C = \bar{C} + c\,\overline{TR} + c(1-t)Y$$
Equation (19)

Full AD:

$$AD = \underbrace{(\bar{C} + c\,\overline{TR} + \bar{I} + G + \overline{NX})}_{\bar{A}} + c(1-t)Y$$
Equation (20)

Note the slope is now $c(1-t)$ — flatter than without taxes because households keep only $(1-t)$ of each income dollar.

6.1 Equilibrium and the Tax-Adjusted Multiplier

Equilibrium with Government
1
$Y = \bar{A} + c(1-t)Y$
2
$Y[1 - c(1-t)] = \bar{A}$
3
$$Y_0 = \frac{\bar{A}}{1-c(1-t)} = \alpha_G\,\bar{A}$$
Equation (21)
$$\boxed{\alpha_G = \frac{1}{1-c(1-t)}}$$
Tax-adjusted multiplier (Equation 23)
No tax (t = 0)

$\alpha = \dfrac{1}{1-c}$

For $c=0.8$: $\alpha = 5$

Tax rate t = 0.25

$\alpha_G = \dfrac{1}{1-0.8(0.75)} = \dfrac{1}{0.4} = 2.5$

Halved by a 25% tax rate!

6.2 Income Taxes as Automatic Stabilisers

Definition

Automatic stabiliser — any mechanism that reduces the change in output in response to a shock in autonomous demand, without discretionary government action.

A proportional income tax is an automatic stabiliser because it lowers the multiplier: adverse investment shocks cause smaller output falls when taxes are in place. Other automatic stabilisers:

  • Unemployment benefits: $TR$ rises automatically when $Y$ falls, sustaining consumption. Reduces the demand contraction.
  • Progressive taxation: Tax burden falls faster in downturns, leaving more disposable income.
Examiner Note
Post-WWII business cycles are less volatile than pre-WWII cycles. Dornbusch attributes this partly to stronger automatic stabilisers (higher income tax rates, unemployment insurance). A 5-mark question may ask you to explain this mechanism — describe how higher $t$ reduces $\alpha_G$ and therefore dampens the income effect of any demand shock.

6.3 Effects of Fiscal Policy Changes

Government spending multiplier:

$$\Delta Y_0 = \alpha_G \cdot \Delta G = \frac{\Delta G}{1-c(1-t)}$$
Equation (22)

Transfer payment multiplier: smaller by factor $c$ (part of transfer is saved):

$$\Delta Y_0 = \alpha_G \cdot c \cdot \Delta\overline{TR}$$

Balanced budget multiplier: If $G$ and $TA$ both rise by the same amount $\Delta G$, the multiplier equals exactly 1 — output rises by $\Delta G$ and no more.

Trap — Transfer vs. Spending Multiplier
Government spending on goods and services ($G$) has a larger multiplier than transfer payments ($TR$). Reason: spending enters AD directly as $\Delta G$. Transfers are first received as income and only a fraction $c$ is spent. Always distinguish these two in exam answers.

7 · The Budget Surplus and Deficit

$$BS = TA - G - \overline{TR} = tY - G - \overline{TR}$$
Equation (24a) — Budget Surplus
Fig 9.3 — Budget Surplus as a Function of Income
The budget surplus is an increasing function of income (slope = $t$). At low incomes, $G + \overline{TR}$ exceeds tax revenue → deficit. At high incomes, tax revenue exceeds spending → surplus. Automatic component: even without policy changes, the deficit rises in recessions.

Key result: an increase in $G$ by $\$1$ raises income by $\alpha_G$ dollars, hence tax revenues by $t \cdot \alpha_G$ dollars. The change in the budget surplus is:

$$\Delta BS = \underbrace{t\,\alpha_G}_{\Delta TA} - \underbrace{1}_{\Delta G} = \frac{t}{1-c(1-t)} - 1 = \frac{-(1-c)(1-t)}{1-c(1-t)} < 0$$
Equation (25) — Government spending always worsens the surplus

For $c=0.8$, $t=0.25$: a $\$1$ increase in $G$ reduces the surplus by $\$0.375$ — not by $\$1$, because multiplied income raises tax collections.

7.1 Full-Employment Budget Surplus

Definition

Full-employment (structural) budget surplus $BS^*$ — the budget surplus that would prevail if output were at potential $Y^*$:

$$BS^* = tY^* - G - \overline{TR}$$
Equation (26)

The difference between actual and full-employment surplus:

$$BS^* - BS = t(Y^* - Y)$$
Equation (27) — Cyclical component of the deficit

In a recession ($Y < Y^*$): actual deficit > full-employment deficit. The excess is the cyclical deficit — it vanishes automatically as the economy recovers. The full-employment surplus measures the discretionary stance of fiscal policy, independent of where the economy sits in the cycle.

Examiner Note
"The government ran a deficit during the 2009 recession — does this mean fiscal policy was expansionary?" Use the full-employment surplus to answer: the cyclical part of a deficit is automatic, not discretionary. Only an increase in the structural (full-employment) deficit indicates a deliberate fiscal expansion. This distinction is frequently tested.

Formula & Concept Summary

Quick Reference

$C = \bar{C} + cY$
Consumption function. $c$ = MPC, $\bar{C}$ = autonomous consumption.
$s = 1 - c$
MPS. MPC + MPS = 1 always.
$AD = \bar{A} + cY$
Aggregate demand. $\bar{A}$ = autonomous spending ($\bar{C}+\bar{I}+G+\overline{NX}+c\cdot\overline{TR}-c\cdot\overline{TA}$).
$Y_0 = \dfrac{\bar{A}}{1-c}$
Equilibrium income (no taxes).
$\alpha = \dfrac{1}{1-c}$
Simple multiplier (no taxes).
$Y_0 = \dfrac{\bar{A}}{1-c(1-t)}$
Equilibrium income with proportional income tax.
$\alpha_G = \dfrac{1}{1-c(1-t)}$
Government multiplier (with taxes). $\alpha_G < \alpha$ always.
$BS = tY - G - \overline{TR}$
Budget surplus. Negative = deficit.
$BS^* = tY^* - G - \overline{TR}$
Full-employment (structural) surplus.
$BS^* - BS = t(Y^* - Y)$
Cyclical component of the deficit.

Revision — Key Distinctions

  • MPC vs. MPS: $c + s = 1$. Not the same thing. A question asking "what is the MPC if the multiplier is 4?" requires $1/(1-c) = 4 \Rightarrow c = 0.75$.
  • Multiplier with taxes: $1/(1-c)$ is the no-tax multiplier. With proportional tax $t$: $\alpha_G = 1/(1-c(1-t))$. Always smaller.
  • $G$ vs. $TR$ multiplier: Government spending enters AD dollar-for-dollar. Transfers are received as income and only fraction $c$ is spent. Transfer multiplier $= c \cdot \alpha_G$.
  • Balanced budget multiplier = 1: Equal increases in $G$ and $TA$ raise income by exactly $\Delta G$.
  • Cyclical vs. structural deficit: Actual deficit = structural deficit + cyclical deficit. Cyclical disappears at $Y^*$.
  • Automatic stabilisers: They work by reducing $\alpha_G$, not by shifting AD. Mechanism: each unit of demand shock generates less income through the multiplier.

Practice Questions

2 MarksConceptual

Define the marginal propensity to consume and explain why it must lie strictly between 0 and 1 for the multiplier to be well-defined and greater than 1.

2 MarksNumerical

If $c = 0.75$ and the income tax rate $t = 0.20$, compute (a) the simple multiplier $\alpha$ and (b) the tax-adjusted multiplier $\alpha_G$. How much does a $\$10$ billion increase in government spending raise equilibrium income?

5 MarksDerivation

Derive the multiplier formula $\alpha = 1/(1-c)$ using (a) the geometric series method and (b) the algebraic equilibrium condition $Y = \bar{A} + cY$. Interpret the result economically: why is a round-by-round spending process equivalent to the formula?

5 MarksDiagram + Analysis

Using the 45° diagram, illustrate and explain the effect of an increase in autonomous investment on equilibrium income. Mark clearly: the initial equilibrium, the new equilibrium, the upward shift of AD, and the multiplied income change. Why is the income increase larger than the investment increase?

5 MarksAnalysis

Explain why proportional income taxes are automatic stabilisers. In your answer: (a) define automatic stabilisers; (b) show mathematically how a tax rate $t$ reduces the multiplier from $\alpha$ to $\alpha_G$; (c) explain the policy implication for business cycle volatility.

10 MarksLong-form Analytical

"In the Keynesian income-expenditure model, fiscal policy is always effective but never fully efficient." Evaluate this statement with reference to: (a) the multiplier mechanism; (b) the difference between $G$ and $TR$ multipliers; (c) the effect of income taxes on the multiplier; (d) the balanced budget multiplier; (e) the impact of government spending on the budget surplus. Use algebra and diagrams to support your arguments.

10 MarksLong-form Analytical

What would a reduction in $G$ do to equilibrium income and to the full-employment surplus?

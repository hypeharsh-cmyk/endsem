CASELET 01 📚 FROM SLIDES

The Production Manager & The Foreman

"Gunjan Ltd's directors asked their production manager to hit 150 TVs/day. The production manager passed this target to his foreman but didn't give him authority to requisition tools and materials from stores. The foreman failed to achieve the target. Can the directors blame the production manager? Can the manager blame his foreman?"
OrganisingDelegationAuthority-ResponsibilityAccountability
solution
  • Yes, the directors CAN blame the production manager. Principle of absoluteness of accountability — accountability cannot be delegated. Even though he passed work down, he remains answerable.
  • No, the production manager CANNOT blame the foreman. Principle of authority and responsibility — giving responsibility without matching authority makes a subordinate ineffective. Foreman couldn't requisition tools, so couldn't deliver.
Authority must always match responsibility. Accountability flows up regardless.
CASELET 02 📚 FROM SLIDES

Home Creations Ltd — Empowered Store Managers

"Home Creations owns 50 department stores. Each store is run autonomously by a manager who decides which products to promote, who to hire, how to handle complaints. This is because local managers know local situations best. The approach has boosted morale and customer service."
OrganisingDecentralisation
solution

Concept = Decentralisation. Systematic delegation of authority to all levels except what must be exercised centrally.

Importance:

  • Develops initiative among subordinates
  • Develops managerial talent (pipeline)
  • Quick decision-making (local people decide locally)

Values communicated: Initiative and Trust.

CASELET 03 📚 FROM SLIDES

Sudhir — Strict but Sociable

"Sudhir is a purchase manager. He's a tough taskmaster demanding strict compliance to rules. But he also chats with subordinates during lunch in the cafeteria — getting insights into their opinions about company policies. Identify the two types of organisation discussed."
OrganisingFormal vs Informal
solution

Formal organisation = structure of authority relationships deliberately created by management (Sudhir enforcing rules and procedures).

Informal organisation = network of social relationships emerging from employee interaction (Sudhir's lunch chats).

Three key differences:

  • Origin: Formal is deliberately created; informal emerges spontaneously.
  • Communication flow: Formal follows scalar chain; informal flows in all directions (grapevine).
  • Purpose: Formal achieves organisational goals; informal fulfils social needs.
CASELET 04 📚 FROM SLIDES

Sewing Machine Co. — Stuck in 1946

"A 1946 sewing machine company follows strict formal organisation structure. Problems: decision-making delays, can't adapt to changing environment, workforce unmotivated, red tapism, high employee turnover. What changes should it bring?"
OrganisingFormal + InformalDiversification
solution
  • Recommendation: Give due importance to informal organisation alongside formal. Enhances job satisfaction, motivates workforce, fixes communication problems.
  • Benefits: Faster information spread, fulfils social needs of employees, fills inadequacies of formal structure.
  • Diversification: Embroidery machines, special machines for ready-made garment manufacturers — uses existing resources optimally.
CASELET 05 📚 FROM SLIDES

Steel Manufacturer's Five Functions

"A steel company has five main jobs: Manufacturing, Finance, Marketing, Personnel, R&D. Which organisational structure should it adopt and why? List six advantages."
OrganisingFunctional Structure
solution

Structure: Functional structure — because activities require high specialisation across distinct functions, and the company is a single product line (steel).

Advantages:

  • Occupational specialisation in each function
  • Promotes control and coordination within each department
  • Increases managerial and operational efficiency → higher profits
  • Minimum duplication of effort
  • Makes employee training easier
  • Ensures each function gets due attention
CASELET 06 🎯 LIKELY

The Growing Startup's Identity Crisis

"BloomTech started as a 15-person startup where the founder personally handled hiring, marketing, and product decisions. Five years later, they have 300 employees across 4 cities. The founder still wants to approve every major decision, but bottlenecks are killing speed. Senior managers are leaving citing 'no autonomy.' New hires are confused about who decides what."
OrganisingCentralisation → DecentralisationScaling
solution
  • Diagnosis: Founder-led centralisation hitting its scaling limit. Worked for 15 people, fails at 300.
  • Recommendation: Systematic decentralisation — disperse decision-making authority to function heads and city managers.
  • Why it matters: (1) relief to founder for strategic focus, (2) quick local decisions, (3) develops next-gen leaders, (4) retains talent by giving them ownership.
  • Complement with clear delegation framework: define authority limits at each level (e.g., spend approvals, hiring approvals, policy changes).
In a fast-changing environment, decision speed at the periphery beats decision quality at the centre.

Staffing caselets.

CASELET 07 📚 FROM SLIDES

Bottom-Up Manpower Estimation

"A company collects requirements: Production 80, Sales 50, HR 20, Finance 30. Top management expects 10% expansion plus 5% reduction due to automation. Estimate total manpower."
StaffingBottom-Up TechniqueCalculation
solution

Method: Bottom-Up Technique. Aggregate departmental requirements, then apply net adjustment.

  • Base = 80 + 50 + 20 + 30 = 180
  • Net change = +10% − 5% = +5%
  • Final = 180 × 1.05 = 189 employees
CASELET 08 📚 FROM SLIDES

Delphi Round Forecasting

"Five HR experts give independent estimates. Round 1: 100, 120, 140, 130, 110. After feedback discussion, Round 2: 115, 125, 135, 128, 120. What's the final manpower estimate?"
StaffingDelphi TechniqueCalculation
solution

Method: Delphi Technique — anonymous iterative expert consensus. Final = average of last round.

  • Sum = 115 + 125 + 135 + 128 + 120 = 623
  • Average = 623 / 5 = 124.6 ≈ 125 employees
CASELET 09 📚 FROM SLIDES

Ratio Analysis with Efficiency Gain

"Past data: 1 employee handles 200 units of output. Expected production = 50,000 units. Efficiency improvement = 10%. Estimate manpower required."
StaffingRatio AnalysisCalculation
solution
  • New productivity = 200 × 1.10 = 220 units/employee
  • Employees needed = 50,000 ÷ 220 = 227.27 ≈ 228 employees
CASELET 10 📚 FROM SLIDES

Markov Workforce Transition

"Workforce: 100 Juniors, 50 Seniors, 20 Managers. Transition probabilities: Junior → Junior 0.7, Senior 0.2, Exit 0.1. Senior → Senior 0.6, Manager 0.3, Exit 0.1. Manager → Manager 0.8, Exit 0.2. Estimate workforce after 1 year."
StaffingMarkov AnalysisCalculation
solution

Method: Markov Analysis — apply transition probabilities level-by-level.

  • Juniors: Stay = 100 × 0.7 = 70 · Promoted = 100 × 0.2 = 20 · Exit = 10
  • Seniors: Stay = 50 × 0.6 = 30 · Promoted = 50 × 0.3 = 15 · Exit = 5
  • Managers: Stay = 20 × 0.8 = 16 · Exit = 4

Final workforce:

  • Junior = 70
  • Senior = 30 + 20 (from promoted Juniors) = 50
  • Manager = 16 + 15 (from promoted Seniors) = 31
CASELET 11 🎯 LIKELY

The Hiring Channel Mix

"A fast-growing IT services firm needs to hire 200 engineers in 6 months. Senior engineers (5+ years) need cultural fit and specialised skills. Freshers can be moulded but need training investment. The HR head debates: campus drives, lateral hiring through portals, employee referrals, or consultancy firms. Budget is tight."
StaffingRecruitment SourcesInternal vs External
solution

Recommendation: Mixed channel strategy, not single-source.

  • Freshers (60-70%) → Campus hiring — high volume, lower cost, cultural malleability. Invest in training.
  • Senior engineers (20-25%) → Job portals + employee referrals — portals widen reach; referrals improve cultural fit (insider vouches).
  • Niche/specialised skills (5-10%) → Consultancies — only when in-house effort fails. Highest cost (~8.33% of CTC).
  • Avoid over-reliance on any single channel — diversification de-risks the hiring funnel.
Channel mix matches role type — freshers from campus, laterals from portals, specialists from consultancies.

Leadership caselets.

CASELET 12 📚 FROM SLIDES

The Silent Supervisor

"Ramesh is a technically strong supervisor but rarely communicates clearly. Instructions are vague ('finish quickly,' 'maintain quality'). Output is up, but rejection rate has climbed sharply. Workers say expectations are unclear. Ramesh insists: 'Workers should know their job.'"
LeadershipCommunication failurePath-Goal (Directive)Control gap
solution
  • Dual failure — (1) leadership communication breakdown, (2) absence of clear performance standards (control gap).
  • Per Path-Goal theory, when tasks are ambiguous, leaders should use directive behaviour — Ramesh is failing this.
  • Action plan for Ramesh: define quality parameters with checklists; introduce daily briefings; use demonstration + feedback loops; set measurable output AND quality targets.
People don't fail because they're incapable; they fail because expectations are unclear.
CASELET 13 📚 FROM SLIDES

The Star Performer Dilemma

"Priya is the top performer at a dairy company — high output, hits targets. But she argues with team members and ignores standard processes. Her manager hesitates to act: 'She delivers results.'"
LeadershipBehaviour vs ResultsAppraisal design
solution
  • Performance = What + How. Ignoring "how" creates toxic culture and norm violations.
  • Inaction signals to the team that bad behaviour is acceptable if results are good — kills team morale.
  • Action: direct behavioural feedback to Priya; link appraisal to BOTH output and teamwork/process adherence; coaching first, corrective action if needed.
Organisations don't just reward results — they reinforce behaviours.
CASELET 14 📚 FROM SLIDES

The Crisis Leader

"An export shipment was rejected for quality issues. The team panicked. The manager avoided communication initially, then blamed workers, didn't involve team in problem-solving. Trust declined. The same issue repeated next month."
LeadershipCrisis responseCommunicationBlame culture
solution
  • Triple failure: communication, ownership, learning.
  • In crisis, a leader must provide direction and reassurance, not silence then blame.
  • Blame culture kills trust AND prevents learning — guaranteeing repeat problems.
  • Correct approach: immediate transparent communication; involve team in root-cause analysis; conduct post-mortem; build process safeguards from learning.
In crisis, leadership is judged not by control, but by response.
CASELET 15 🎯 LIKELY

The Two Founders

"Founders A and B run competing fintechs. Founder A is hands-on, sets weekly goals, rewards top performers monthly, micro-manages execution. Founder B inspires teams with a 10-year vision, mentors employees personally, encourages questioning the status quo. A's company grows steadily. B's team is fewer but produces breakthrough products. The board wants to know which leadership style is 'right.'"
LeadershipTransactionalTransformational
solution
  • Founder A = Transactional leader — exchange-based, rewards/structure-driven, clear roles. Great for stable execution, but limited in inspiring breakthrough thinking.
  • Founder B = Transformational leader — vision-driven, develops people, encourages innovation. Slower steady-state growth, but enables paradigm shifts.
  • Neither is universally "right": stage of company, team maturity, and market context determine fit. Transactional fits scaling/steady operations; Transformational fits innovation-heavy or crisis-recovery phases.
  • Best companies often combine both — transactional for systems, transformational for culture and direction.
CASELET 16 🎯 LIKELY

The New Team Lead

"Akash is promoted to lead a 6-person analytics team. Two members are seasoned experts (8+ years). Two are mid-level (3 years). Two are fresh graduates (6 months in). Akash decides to apply the same leadership style with everyone — give detailed instructions, check progress hourly. The senior members start disengaging. The freshers seem comfortable but slow."
LeadershipSituational LeadershipHersey-Blanchard
solution
  • Diagnosis: Akash is applying uniform leadership where situational/contingency theory demands adaptation to follower readiness.
  • Senior members (high ability, high willingness) → use Delegating style. Give autonomy.
  • Mid-level (moderate readiness) → Participative style. Consult them, share decisions.
  • Freshers (low ability, willing to learn) → Directive/Selling style. Detailed instructions, close coaching.
  • Per Path-Goal: directive for ambiguous tasks, supportive for stress, participative for experts, achievement-oriented for high-potential.
One leadership style fits no team. Read the readiness, match the style.

Controlling caselets.

CASELET 17 📚 FROM SLIDES

Gagan & The 20-Unit Gap

"Gagan lets his team perform freely. He then calls a meeting and asks them to check what they've achieved. One day, output is 20 units below target. He declares no worker leaves until the 20-unit gap is closed."
ControllingControl ProcessDeviationPlanning-Control link
solution
  • Function: Controlling.
  • What is '20'? The deviation — gap between actual performance and set standards.
  • When was the target set? At the planning stage. Planning sets standards; controlling minimises deviations from them.
Planning and controlling are bookends — one sets the standard, the other enforces it.

CASELET 19 📚 FROM SLIDES

Parasmani Ship Builders — Multiple Frustrations

"Parasmani is doing well but employees complain there are no standards to judge motivation or job satisfaction. HR is struggling. Other control efforts feel expensive. Workers resist computer monitoring and CCTVs, calling it against their freedom. Despite all controls, a competitor with better technology has caused losses."
ControllingLimitations of Control
solution

Concept: Limitations of controlling. The case highlights all four:

  • Difficult to set quantitative standards for qualitative things (motivation, job satisfaction).
  • Controlling is costly (monitoring systems, CCTVs).
  • Faces resistance from employees who see surveillance as freedom-violation.
  • Little control over external factors (competitor entering with better tech).
CASELET 20 📚 FROM SLIDES

Peter & The Tyre Machine

"Peter is production manager. Target = 80 tyres/day. Actual = 65. Beyond acceptable range. He also notices a marginal increase in office stationery expenses, and a machine performing poorly. How should he prioritise?"
ControllingCritical Point ControlManagement by Exception
solution
  • Critical Point Control: Focus on Key Result Areas. Peter should prioritise the machine performance (KRA — drives output). Stationery expense is non-critical; address later.
  • Management by Exception: The 15-unit shortfall is beyond acceptable range, so it MUST be escalated. The stationery rise is within normal range, so it doesn't need management attention.
  • Action: Repair or replace the machine immediately; the stationery deviation can be tracked routinely.
CASELET 21 📚 FROM SLIDES

Quality Issues at Swift Manufacturing

"Swift makes electronic components for phones. Issues: weak raw material quality checks, no supplier standards (feedforward weak). Machines run without real-time monitoring; workers skip intermediate checks under pressure (concurrent weak). Defects discovered only after shipment; customer complaints rising (feedback only)."
ControllingFeedforward · Concurrent · Feedback
solution
  • Weakest type: Feedforward control. No supplier standards, weak incoming material checks, no design-stage prevention.
  • Problems from weak feedforward: defective inputs enter production → defects in final goods → higher rework, wastage, cost → customer complaints, brand damage.
  • Concurrent fix: introduce real-time monitoring; automated quality checks; train workers to detect/fix defects immediately; supervisor checks at every stage; stop defective production early.
Effective control = feedforward (prevent) + concurrent (correct in real-time) + feedback (learn). All three.
CASELET 22 🎯 LIKELY

The Balanced Scorecard Pushback

"A mid-sized FMCG company has rewarded managers purely on quarterly financial metrics for years. Profit is up, but customer complaints are rising, employee turnover hit 28%, and the innovation pipeline is empty. The CFO insists 'financials are the truth.' The CHRO is proposing a new performance system."
ControllingBalanced Scorecard
solution
  • Diagnosis: Single-dimensional control (only financial) is starving the other three dimensions critical to long-term performance.
  • Recommendation: Adopt the Balanced Scorecard — track four dimensions equally:
    • Financial: revenue, profit, cost.
    • Customer: satisfaction, retention, complaint rate.
    • Internal processes: defect rate, cycle time, on-time delivery.
    • Learning & growth: employee engagement, training hours, innovation output.
  • Why it works: overweighting finance now is depleting customer goodwill, talent, and future capability. Balance is what makes financial performance sustainable.
What you measure is what you incentivise — measure only finance, lose everything else that makes finance possible.

Motivation caselets.

CASELET 23 📚 FROM SLIDES

Ravi, Neha & Arjun

"Ravi (top performer) is missing deadlines and seems withdrawn. Neha shows up but contributes the bare minimum. Arjun is high-potential and considering leaving. The manager applies the same motivation policy — increased bonuses — to all three. None of them improve."
MotivationIntrinsic vs ExtrinsicAdaptive management
solution

Root-cause diagnosis (each is different):

  • Ravi: Burnout from chronic overload. Needs workload reduction + recovery time, not bonuses.
  • Neha: Disengagement from lack of meaning. Needs ownership of a visible project + job enrichment, not money.
  • Arjun: Growth frustration from underutilisation. Needs stretch assignment + mentorship + fast-track path.

Urgency ranking:

  • 1. Ravi — burnout risk, performance collapse imminent.
  • 2. Arjun — attrition risk for high-potential talent.
  • 3. Neha — stable but stagnant; lower immediate risk.
Employees don't fail from the same reason — diagnose the driver before prescribing the fix.
CASELET 24 🎯 LIKELY

The Money Myth

"A food company manager believes 'money is the only real motivator.' He raises incentives 20%. Short-term output jumps. But after 3 months: quality drops, employees focus only on quantity, teamwork declines, two team leads quit citing 'transactional culture.'"
MotivationIntrinsic vs ExtrinsicIncentive design
solution
  • Diagnosis: Over-reliance on extrinsic motivation (money) at the cost of intrinsic drivers (mastery, meaning, autonomy).
  • The trap: incentives drive what is measured (quantity), not what is desired (quality, teamwork).
  • Better approach:
    • Balanced incentives across quantity + quality + teamwork.
    • Recognition programs alongside cash.
    • Growth opportunities and skill development.
    • Autonomy in how work gets done.
What you reward is what you get — often at the cost of everything else.

Signaling caselets.

CASELET 25 🎯 LIKELY

The Brand-Name Hiring Trap

"A tech firm hires 80% of its analysts from top 5 institutes. Recent data shows performance variance within these hires is high — some are stars, others underperform. Meanwhile, several rejected candidates from tier-2 colleges who joined competitors are outperforming. The HR director wants to redesign the screening process."
HRM / StaffingSignaling TheoryHiring bias
solution
  • Concept: Michael Spence's Signaling Theory. Employers face information asymmetry — they can't directly observe productivity, so they rely on signals (degree, institute).
  • Problem: Signals are imperfect proxies. A top-institute degree signals discipline and intelligence but doesn't cause productivity. Over-reliance creates bias — talented non-institute hires get filtered out.
  • Redesign recommendations:
    • Combine signals with skill-based assessments (coding tests, case studies, work simulations).
    • Use behavioural interviews to evaluate problem-solving and culture fit.
    • Track post-hire performance by source — let data correct the bias.
    • Build employer branding as a counter-signal to attract talent beyond the resume circuit.
Signals get you the interview. Skills get you the job. Don't confuse the two.
CASELET 26 🎯 LIKELY

The Credential Inflation Problem

"A consulting firm's entry-level role required a Bachelor's degree 10 years ago. Today, candidates routinely come with MBAs, multiple certifications, and overseas internships. Recruiters increasingly demand more credentials to filter. But onboarding shows new hires aren't proportionally more productive than past hires. Costs are up, hiring takes 4x longer."
HRM / StaffingSignaling TheorySignal inflation
solution
  • Concept: Signal inflation — when everyone has a degree, the degree stops differentiating, so candidates pile on more credentials, and recruiters demand more to filter.
  • The trap: credentials grow, but actual productivity doesn't. Cost and time inflate; signal value deflates.
  • Recommendations:
    • Decouple credentials from screening — define the actual skills needed for the role.
    • Use structured assessments (case work, technical tests) that measure capability directly.
    • Pilot skill-first hiring with smaller candidate pools and track outcomes.
    • Recognise that signals fail at the margin — the 10th certification doesn't predict performance better than the 2nd.

Integrator caselets.

CASELET 27 🎯 LIKELY

Great on Paper, Broken in Practice

"GreenHarvest Foods has well-documented HR policies, a clean org chart, ISO-certified processes, and an annual appraisal system. Yet employees report confusion about priorities, unclear daily expectations, low motivation, delayed feedback, and frustration with managers who don't communicate during pressure. The CEO is puzzled — 'We've designed everything correctly.'"
All five functionsManagement in ActionFormal vs Lived
solution
  • Diagnosis: Classic gap between formal system (written) and lived reality (executed). Management isn't designed in documents — it's lived in daily interactions.
  • Function-by-function diagnosis:
    • Planning: Long-term plans exist; weekly task clarity doesn't. Fix: clear deadlines, communicated expectations.
    • Organising: Chart is clear; coordination between teams is weak. Fix: lateral communication norms.
    • Staffing: Hiring works; onboarding and integration don't. Fix: deep mentoring, role clarity.
    • Leadership: Managers manage tasks but don't communicate under pressure. Fix: daily check-ins, supportive behaviour.
    • Control: Annual appraisal exists; real-time feedback doesn't. Fix: monthly/quarterly check-ins, concurrent feedback.
  • Strengthening daily execution at the manager-employee interface fixes more than redesigning policies ever will.
Management is not what's written in handbooks — it's what happens between people at work every single day.
CASELET 28 🎯 LIKELY

The McKinsey 7S Stress Test

"AeroParts Ltd had a strong 20-year run as a precision aerospace components manufacturer. Recently, the board hired an aggressive new CEO from a fast-moving consumer goods background. He's pushed an ambitious growth strategy, but employees say the new style clashes with their precision-craftsmanship culture. The old reporting structure was disrupted in a restructuring, IT systems weren't updated, and skilled engineers are leaving. Quality slipped, growth stalled."
OrganisingMcKinsey 7SMisalignment
solution

7S Audit:

  • Strategy — newly aggressive (growth-focused). ⚠️ doesn't fit precision-craftsmanship industry.
  • Structure — recently disrupted, unclear after restructuring. WEAK.
  • Systems — IT not updated to match new strategy. WEAK.
  • Shared Values — precision craftsmanship culture clashing with FMCG-speed style. WEAK.
  • Style — aggressive new CEO style alien to the org. WEAK.
  • Staff — skilled engineers exiting. WEAKENING.
  • Skills — historically strong, but eroding with attrition.

Diagnosis: Multiple soft S's misaligned (Style + Shared Values) dragging hard S's (Structure + Systems) down. Strategy is now incompatible with the rest.

Recommendation:

  • Realign Style first — CEO must adapt approach or the culture clash will continue bleeding talent.
  • Stabilise Structure and update Systems before chasing growth.
  • Reinforce Shared Values explicitly — celebrate precision and craftsmanship.
  • Only THEN re-evaluate Strategy — aggressive growth may need pacing.
Culture eats strategy for breakfast. One misaligned S can topple the rest.
